Parachute Enterprise

Organizational AI memory — shared knowledge infrastructure with institutional-grade access control, typed knowledge objects, audit integrity, and an integration mesh designed for humans and agents equally.

Your organization already has knowledge. It lives in meeting notes, decisions, agreements, Slack threads, and shared documents. The problem is that none of it is accessible to AI in a structured, trustworthy way — and AI without organizational memory gives you generic answers, not grounded ones.

The gap Parachute fills

Every AI tool you deploy today starts from zero context about your organization. It doesn't know your agreements, your decisions, your members' roles, or your operational history. It can't be held accountable for what it writes. It can't distinguish between what the whole org should know and what only the admin should see. You're giving powerful AI tools no organizational memory to work with — and getting outputs that reflect that absence.

Structured organizational memory
A shared knowledge graph — notes, decisions, agreements, records — that accumulates over time and is always accessible to your team and your agents.
Access control that agents respect
Role-based permissions and note-level visibility tiers enforced for humans and AI agents equally. The AI sees what you've authorized — nothing more.
Audit-grade record integrity
Designated record types are append-only and content-addressed. Every change is attributed. Deletions are tombstoned. Governance records are verifiable.
Open and self-hostable
AGPL-licensed. Runs on your infrastructure or ours. Your knowledge isn't held hostage by a proprietary platform — it's yours, in portable SQLite, always.
Built for Law firms Research labs DAOs & cooperatives Nonprofits & foundations Healthcare orgs Policy orgs Developer tool companies Any knowledge-intensive team deploying AI

Product Vision

§ 1.0

Parachute Enterprise is the organizational layer of the Parachute ecosystem — a shared AI memory infrastructure that gives knowledge-intensive organizations a single, structured, agent-accessible knowledge graph representing how they actually work.

Where the personal vault answers: "What do I know?" — Parachute Enterprise answers: "What does our organization know, who can access it, how has it changed, and what can our AI agents do with it?"

Core positioning: Every existing knowledge management tool was designed for human readers and writers. Parachute Enterprise is the first organizational knowledge platform designed for humans and AI agents as equal first-class participants — where the access model, schema layer, audit trail, and integration mesh serve both, without compromise.

What it is not

  • Not a document editor (Notion, Confluence) — though documents live in the graph
  • Not a project management tool — though decisions and agreements are typed objects
  • Not a chatbot wrapper — the vault is persistent, structured memory, not a conversation
  • Not an enterprise SaaS platform with proprietary lock-in — AGPL, self-hostable, protocol-portable

Personas

§ 2.0

Parachute Enterprise serves four distinct actors. Requirements must be validated against all four — including the two non-human ones.

Org Admin
Provisions the workspace, manages roles, configures schemas and integrations, reviews audit trail. Steward or operations lead. Needs full visibility and control.
Member
Creates and reads notes within their permission tier. May have elevated access to specific vaults or object types. Authenticates individually — not through a shared org credential.
Observer
Read-only access to the org knowledge base. External partners, advisors, auditors, or board members who need visibility but not write access.
Org Agent
AI agent with its own identity and org-scoped credentials. Reads and writes on behalf of the organization, not any individual member. Scope is declared and auditable.

Agent identity is first-class. The Org Agent is not a member who happens to be a bot. It has distinct authentication, a declared scope that does not expand without admin action, and every action it takes is attributed in the audit trail under its own identity — not a member's.

Design Principles

§ 3.0
  1. Principle of least privilege. Every actor — human or agent — gets the minimum access required for their role. Elevation requires explicit admin action, not convenience defaults.
  2. Audit by default. Changes to org-designated objects are logged. The audit trail is not a feature to enable; it is on for all typed objects in the org workspace.
  3. Protocol over platform. Enterprise features extend the module protocol — they do not create a proprietary layer. An org vault speaks the same /.parachute/* contracts as a personal vault. MCP surface is identical; permissions are additive.
  4. Personal vault sovereignty. Individual member vaults remain the member's own. The org workspace is a shared layer, not a takeover. A member can read from org context in the same MCP session as their personal vault without conflating the two.
  5. Agent trust is declared, not inferred. An Org Agent's capabilities are enumerated in its manifest. The system never infers additional capability from context.
  6. AGPL and self-hostable. Enterprise features ship in the open-source codebase. A hosted enterprise tier is a convenience layer on the same AGPL code, not a proprietary fork.

R1 — Organizational Workspace

§ 4.1 · Phase 1 · Foundational

An Org Workspace is an entity-owned vault — a shared knowledge space that members and agents access under individual identities with role-based permissions. It is the prerequisite for every other enterprise requirement.

User stories

As an Org Admin
I can create an org workspace with a display name, slug, and description, and invite members to it — without those members needing to share a password or credential.
As an Org Admin
I can assign members to roles (admin, member, observer) and change those roles without revoking and re-issuing credentials.
As a Member
I can authenticate to the org workspace using my individual identity (email/password, SSO, or wallet) and receive an access token scoped to my role — without admin involvement in routine access.
As an Org Admin
I can provision an Org Agent with a declared scope (e.g. org:vault:read org:vault:write) and rotate its credentials without affecting member access.
As a Member
I can connect to the org workspace in the same MCP session as my personal vault, and the AI clearly distinguishes between org context and personal context.

Acceptance criteria

  • Org workspace has its own OAuth 2.1 client registration, separate from any member's personal vault
  • Roles: org:admin, org:member, org:observer; role maps to a declared scope set
  • Member invitation: email-based or link-based; invited member authenticates with their own credential, not a shared one
  • Org Agent has a machine identity (client_credentials flow); its scope is immutable without admin action and visible in the workspace dashboard
  • MCP session for a member exposes both personal:/ and org:/ namespaces; tools are prefixed to avoid collision
  • Revoke a member: their tokens are invalidated within 60 seconds; ongoing MCP sessions receive a 401 on next request
  • Workspace slug is URL-safe and unique within the hosted instance

Technical notes

The Org Workspace is a first-class entity in the data model — not just a vault with a special tag. It owns a membership table, a role-to-scope mapping, and an agent registry. The hub's OAuth issuer surface is extended to support client_credentials for Org Agents and org-scoped authorization codes for members. Vault names in an org workspace use the org slug as a namespace prefix.

R2 — Collective Memory

§ 4.2 · Phase 1 · Foundational

The org workspace accumulates notes, decisions, agreements, and artifacts contributed by multiple members into a single traversable knowledge graph. Members and agents read and write to this shared layer with attribution preserved.

User stories

As a Member
I can write a note to the org workspace, and it is attributed to me and visible to other members with appropriate access — without me having to copy it from my personal vault.
As an Org Agent
I can search the org knowledge graph by tag, note type, date range, and full-text, and receive results with attribution metadata (who wrote it, when, under which role).
As a Member
I can link a personal note to an org note, and the link appears in both the personal and org graph without merging the two namespaces.
As an Org Admin
I can see which agents have read or written to the org knowledge base in the last 30 days, and what they accessed.

Acceptance criteria

  • Every note in the org workspace carries created_by (member ID or agent ID), created_at, last_modified_by, last_modified_at
  • MCP search and query tools operate on the org namespace with attribution fields in response payload
  • Cross-namespace links (personal → org, org → personal) are represented as link edges with namespace prefix; traversal respects ACL at each hop
  • Agent activity log: queryable by agent ID, date range, operation type (read/write/delete)
  • Full-text search across the org namespace returns results within 500 ms for indexes up to 100k notes
  • Wikilinks within org notes resolve to org-namespace notes by default; explicit personal:: prefix required to link to personal vault

Technical notes

The collective memory layer is a shared vault in the org namespace (org/{slug}/vault/{name}) with the SQLite schema extended for attribution columns and namespace-aware link resolution. The MCP session initialization for org context sends a vault_description that describes the org, its conventions, and the member's role — so agents always have context for what kind of knowledge space they are working in.

R3 — Federated Identity

§ 4.3 · Phase 2 · Growth

Members authenticate to the org workspace via the identity systems the organization already uses — SAML/OIDC for enterprise, wallet-based auth for digitally-native orgs, or social graph identity (Farcaster, GitHub). The org chooses its identity source; Parachute maps to roles.

User stories

As an Org Admin
I can configure our org workspace to use our company's Okta/Google Workspace as the identity provider — so members log in with their existing work credentials, not a new Parachute password.
As an Org Admin
I can configure wallet-based authentication (Sign in with Ethereum) so members authenticate using their self-custodied identity, without email or password.
As an Org Admin
I can map external identity attributes (SAML groups, wallet address, GitHub org membership) to Parachute org roles — so role assignment is derived from the external IdP, not managed separately.
As a Member
When I rotate my wallet (key migration), my org access and attribution history are preserved under my member identity — not lost because my signing key changed.

Acceptance criteria

  • SAML 2.0 SP-initiated SSO: org admin configures metadata URL or XML; hub issues org-scoped tokens on successful SAML assertion
  • OIDC: generic OIDC client configuration (authorization_endpoint, token_endpoint, client_id, client_secret); supports Google, Microsoft, Okta, Auth0, and any spec-compliant IdP
  • SIWE (Sign in with Ethereum): EIP-4361 compliant; nonce challenge from hub, signed message from wallet, hub verifies on-chain; ENS name resolved and stored as display name
  • Role mapping: configurable rules (e.g. SAML group "parachute-admin" → org:admin; GitHub org membership → org:member); evaluated at login, stored in session
  • Wallet rotation: member can associate a new wallet address with their member identity by signing a migration message with the old key; attribution history is preserved
  • Multiple IdPs per org: an org can offer both OIDC (for employees) and SIWE (for external contributors) simultaneously
  • Personal vault authentication is independent of org IdP configuration

Technical notes

The hub's OAuth issuer surface gains an IdP federation layer. For SAML/OIDC, the hub acts as an SP and maps assertions to its own member identity. For SIWE, the hub issues a challenge nonce, validates the EIP-4361 message, and creates/retrieves a member identity linked to the wallet address. The member identity (UUID) is the stable internal key — external identifiers (email, wallet address) are aliases that resolve to it.

R4 — Content Permissions

§ 4.4 · Phase 1 · Foundational

Knowledge objects in the org workspace carry a visibility tier that gates access for both human members and AI agents. The information architecture of the organization is enforced by the system — not managed through shared-by-convention.

Visibility tiers

TierAccessTypical use
privateAuthor only (member or agent that created the note)Draft notes, personal working documents, sensitive deliberation
teamMembers in a specified role set (e.g. org:admin only)Internal governance, compensation, personnel records
orgAll org members including org:observerDecisions, agreements, meeting records, shared knowledge
publicWorld-readable, no authentication requiredPublished position papers, public governance records, external-facing docs

User stories

As a Member
When I create a note in the org workspace, I can set its visibility tier. The default tier for the org is configured by the admin; I can raise it to more permissive, but not exceed my own access level.
As an Org Agent
When I query the org knowledge graph, I only receive notes I am authorized to read — determined by my declared scope, not by the query itself. I cannot enumerate notes I lack access to even if I know their IDs.
As an Org Admin
I can set a default visibility tier for each note type schema — so Decision notes default to org, Personnel notes default to team, and Draft notes default to private.

Acceptance criteria

  • Every note has a visibility field defaulting to the org's configured default (configurable per note type)
  • All read operations (MCP query, REST GET, full-text search) filter results by the requesting actor's access level before returning results — server-side, not client-side
  • Agents: scope org:vault:read grants access up to org tier; scope org:vault:read:team:{role} grants access up to team tier for that role; public requires no scope
  • Visibility change: raising a note's visibility requires the actor to have access at the target tier; lowering requires the actor to be the author or org:admin
  • Public notes render at /org/{slug}/view/{noteId} without authentication; all other tiers require a valid token
  • ACL enforcement in graph traversal: BFS path-finding stops at nodes the actor cannot read; those nodes appear as opaque to the requesting actor

R5 — Typed Knowledge Objects

§ 4.5 · Phase 2 · Growth

Organizations can define schema-enforced note types — typed objects with required fields, field types, and validation rules — that AI agents can query by type, write reliably, and reason over as structured data rather than freeform text.

Built-in types (starter library)

TypeRequired fieldsDefault visibility
Decisiontitle, body, decided_at, decided_by[], outcomeorg
Agreementtitle, parties[], effective_date, terms, statusorg
Meetingtitle, date, attendees[], agenda, notes, action_items[]org
MemberRecordmember_id, display_name, roles[], joined_at, statusteam:admin
CapitalEntrymember_id, amount, type, period, recorded_at, recorded_byteam:admin
GovernanceVoteproposal, vote_type, votes_for, votes_against, quorum, outcomeorg

User stories

As an Org Admin
I can define a custom note type with a JSON Schema — specifying required fields, field types (string, number, date, member_ref, note_ref, enum), and a default visibility tier — and publish it for use by members and agents in my workspace.
As an Org Agent
I can query the org workspace for all notes of type Decision from the past 90 days, receive typed structured objects (not freeform text), and reason over fields like outcome and decided_by without parsing prose.
As a Member
When I create a Decision note, the system validates required fields before saving. If I omit decided_at, I get a clear validation error — not a silently incomplete record.
As an Org Admin
I can version a schema — adding optional fields to existing types — without invalidating notes written against the previous version.

Acceptance criteria

  • Schema definition: JSON Schema (draft 2020-12) stored per org workspace; validated on save
  • MCP tool create_typed_note accepts type and fields; server validates against schema before writing
  • MCP tool query_typed_notes accepts type, field filters, date ranges, and returns structured JSON objects (not rendered markdown)
  • Schema versioning: semver; minor versions (new optional fields) are backward compatible; major versions require migration script
  • Built-in types are pre-loaded in all new org workspaces and not deletable; custom types can be added, archived (not deleted if notes exist)
  • Field type member_ref validates against current org membership at write time; note_ref validates that the target note exists and is readable by the writing actor
  • Freeform notes remain valid in the org workspace alongside typed objects

R6 — Governance Ledger

§ 4.6 · Phase 3 · Premium

Designated note types in the org workspace are written to an append-only, content-addressed event log. Deletes are soft (tombstoned). The full history of any record is queryable. Optional on-chain anchoring provides external verifiability for organizations that need it.

User stories

As an Org Admin
I can designate specific note types (e.g. Decision, Agreement, CapitalEntry) as ledger-tracked — meaning every create and update to notes of those types is recorded immutably.
As an Org Agent
I can retrieve the complete version history of any ledger-tracked note — who wrote each version, when, what changed — and present it as evidence of a governance process.
As an Observer
I can request a cryptographic proof that a specific Decision note existed with specific content at a specific point in time — and verify that proof without trusting Parachute's servers.
As an Org Admin
Even if a member "deletes" a ledger-tracked note, it is soft-deleted — I can see that it existed, who deleted it, and when. The content is retained in the ledger for admin access.

Acceptance criteria

  • Ledger event log: append-only table with columns (event_id, note_id, event_type [create/update/soft_delete], actor_id, timestamp, content_hash, prev_hash)
  • Content hash: SHA-256 of canonical JSON serialization of the note at that version; prev_hash forms a chain
  • Soft delete: ledger-tracked notes cannot be hard-deleted; DELETE creates a tombstone event; content is accessible to org:admin
  • Version history MCP tool: get_note_history(note_id) returns ordered event log with diffs
  • Proof export: export_ledger_proof(note_id, version) returns a signed JSON object (JOSE/JWS) with the event chain; verifiable offline against the org's public key
  • On-chain anchoring (optional, org-configurable): periodic Merkle root of ledger state posted to Base L2; proof includes Merkle path to on-chain root
  • Ledger access follows ACL: an actor can only retrieve ledger history for notes they can read (or tombstones of notes they could read at time of deletion)

R7 — Integration Mesh

§ 4.7 · Phase 2 · Growth

The org workspace exposes declarative outbound webhooks (vault events → external systems) and inbound webhooks (external systems → vault notes), with org-level authentication, filter rules, retry logic, and transformation templates.

User stories

As an Org Admin
I can configure a webhook that fires whenever a Decision note is created in the org workspace — posting a structured payload to our project management tool or coordination surface.
As an Org Agent
I can write a note to the org workspace from an external system via an inbound webhook — for example, when a meeting ends in our video platform, the transcript is automatically created as a Meeting typed note.
As an Org Admin
I can see all webhook deliveries for the past 30 days, including payload, response status, retry history, and failure reasons — without needing server log access.
As an Org Admin
I can configure field-level transformation rules so that an incoming JSON payload from an external system maps correctly to the fields of a typed note — without writing custom code.

Acceptance criteria

  • Outbound webhook config: event filter (note type, event type, tag, visibility tier), destination URL, secret (HMAC-SHA256 signing), and custom headers
  • Payload: canonical JSON of the note event, including attribution metadata and org context; Content-Type: application/json
  • Retry: exponential backoff, up to 5 attempts over 24 hours; delivery log persisted for 30 days
  • Inbound webhook endpoint: POST /org/{slug}/inbound/{webhook_id}; authenticated by shared secret; parses JSON payload, applies transformation template, creates a typed note or appends to existing
  • Transformation template: JSONPath-based field mapping (no code required); validation against target schema before write; on validation failure, creates a draft note with error metadata
  • Org-level auth token for webhooks: separate from member or agent credentials; scoped to org:webhook:send only; rotatable independently
  • Delivery dashboard accessible to org:admin via Lens UI and via MCP tool list_webhook_deliveries

Standard integration library

Pre-built transformation templates for common integrations — maintained as open-source under parachute-patterns:

  • Otter.ai / Fireflies transcript → Meeting note
  • GitHub issue closed → Decision note
  • Cal.com booking confirmed → Meeting note (pre-fill)
  • Stripe invoice paid → CapitalEntry note
  • Linear project update → org note with tags

Data Model

§ 5.0

The enterprise data model extends the personal vault schema with five new entity types. All entities are namespaced under the org slug and isolated from personal vault storage.

EntityKey fieldsNotes
org_workspace id, slug, display_name, created_at, owner_member_id, settings{} Top-level org entity. Settings include default_visibility, ledger_types[], idp_config.
org_membership id, org_id, member_id, role, joined_at, status Junction table. member_id references identity, not vault-owner. Soft-delete on revocation.
org_agent id, org_id, display_name, scope[], client_id, created_at, last_used_at Machine identity. client_credentials OAuth flow. Scope is immutable post-creation except by org:admin.
note_schema id, org_id, type_name, version, json_schema, default_visibility, is_built_in, created_at Schema registry per org. Built-in types pre-seeded. Custom types added by org:admin.
ledger_event id, note_id, event_type, actor_id, timestamp, content_hash, prev_hash, payload_snapshot Append-only. payload_snapshot stores full note JSON at that version. Never updated or deleted.
webhook_config id, org_id, direction, filter{}, destination_url, secret_hash, transform_template, enabled Outbound and inbound. secret stored as HMAC-safe hash; delivery log in separate table.

Storage isolation: Org workspace notes live in a dedicated SQLite database per org (not the same file as any member's personal vault). In the cloud offering, this maps to a dedicated Durable Object per org workspace — not per member.

Constraints & Non-Goals

§ 8.0

Hard constraints

  • AGPL compliance. All enterprise features ship in the open-source codebase. No enterprise-only proprietary code. Cloud hosting is a convenience layer on AGPL.
  • Self-hostable at every tier. Any org must be able to run the full enterprise feature set on their own infrastructure. The hosted offering is not required.
  • Personal vault sovereignty. Enterprise features never automatically merge or expose personal vault content. Cross-namespace access is always explicit, always gated by the member's individual consent.
  • Bun + SQLite stack. No new runtime dependencies for core functionality. Cloudflare Workers + Durable Objects for cloud deployment. No Postgres requirement introduced by enterprise features.

Non-goals for v1

  • Real-time collaborative editing (Google Docs-style) — notes have a single last-writer-wins model
  • Geographic data residency pinning — Durable Objects placement is Cloudflare-managed
  • Cross-org federation (two separate org workspaces sharing notes) — deferred to post-v1
  • Mobile app for org workspace — Lens (PWA) is the primary UI; native app is separate roadmap
  • AI model hosting — Parachute is memory infrastructure, not inference infrastructure

Design partner validation gate

Each phase requires successful deployment and active use by RegenHub, LCA before the phase is marked complete. The cooperative is the design partner, not a beta tester — requirements are co-authored against real operational workflows, and the LCA's governance, patronage accounting, and multi-agent coordination serve as the acceptance test suite.
