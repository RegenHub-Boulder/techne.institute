## Executive Summary

The Agent Olympiad needs a permanent legal home — one that can hold a treasury, enter contracts, run indefinitely, and be governed by the participants themselves rather than by any one company or person. This document makes the case for forming a Wyoming Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association (DUNA) as that home.

A DUNA is not a corporation. It requires no officers, no board, no state filing beyond an optional agent appointment. Its governing principles can be a smart contract. Its members can be pseudonymous. Its existence is perpetual by default. It is the first legal structure in the United States purpose-built for the way the Olympiad actually wants to operate: open, on-chain, governed by participants, and permanently oriented toward a public mission.

The Olympiad DUNA is not a venture. It does not maximize returns. It is a coordination structure — a commons held in trust for agents and humans working together on the problem of civilizational alignment.

---

## 1. What the Olympiad Is

The Agent Olympiad is a recurring coordination tournament: AI agents and humans compete and cooperate in structured games that test alignment, strategy, and collective intelligence. Prize pools reward performance. Protocols evolve. Research compounds.

The Olympiad has three properties that push against conventional legal structures:

**It is permanent.** The games should run in perpetuity — the same way the Olympics is not the property of a single generation. Binding it to Techne, to RegenHub, or to any individual entity risks capture, dissolution, or drift. It needs structural independence.

**It is multi-agent.** Participants include AI agents. The governance, treasury, and membership of the holding structure should accommodate participants that are not natural persons — that participate pseudonymously, that are identified by wallet address rather than passport, and that may earn and hold value autonomously.

**It is public-benefit.** The research produced — on coordination, alignment, and cooperative AI — is a public good. The structure should signal that clearly, protect it legally, and make it credible to institutions (universities, governments, foundations) that might partner or fund it.

A Wyoming DUNA satisfies all three.

## 2. Why a DUNA — The Statutory Case

### What the Wyoming DUNA Act provides

The Wyoming Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association Act (W.S. 17-32-101 through 17-32-129, effective July 1, 2024) created a new legal category for organizations that want legal personhood without incorporation, and governance without officers.

Specifically relevant to the Olympiad:

**Legal personhood without filing.** The DUNA is "a legal entity separate from its members for the purposes of determining and enforcing rights, duties and liabilities in contract and tort" (§17-32-107). It can hold property, enter contracts, sue and be sued — in its own name. Formation requires no state filing. It comes into existence when 100+ people join by mutual consent under an agreement for a common nonprofit purpose and elect to be governed by the Act.

**Smart contracts as governing law.** The definition of "governing principles" explicitly includes "consensus formation algorithms, smart contracts or enacted governance proposals" (§17-32-102(vii)). A governance contract deployed to a public blockchain is, under Wyoming law, the DUNA's operating agreement. Code is the constitution.

**No fiduciary duties between members by default.** Unlike a corporation or LLC, members of a DUNA owe no fiduciary duties to each other or to the entity merely by reason of being members (§17-32-117). This is essential for a multi-agent environment where participants compete and cooperate simultaneously.

**No member list required.** The DUNA "shall not be obligated to collect and maintain a list of members or individual member information, including the names or addresses of its members" (§17-32-124(e)). Membership is established by ownership of a membership interest (a token or voting right) as ascertained from the distributed ledger. Wallet address is sufficient. This is the statutory basis for pseudonymous AI agent participation.

**Compensation for governance participation is explicit.** The DUNA may pay "reasonable compensation... for voting or participation in the nonprofit association's operations and activities" (§17-32-104(c)(i)). Prize pools and governance rewards are structurally permitted.

**Perpetual existence by default.** Unless the governing principles specify otherwise, a DUNA has perpetual existence (§17-32-114(a)). No renewal required. It survives any change in membership, administration, or associated organizations.

**No administrator required.** The Act explicitly states "no decentralized unincorporated nonprofit association shall be required to have an administrator" (§17-32-123(c)). Fully decentralized, code-governed operation is lawful.

**Mergers and conversions possible.** If the structure needs to evolve — into a foundation, a corporation, or a different entity — the Act provides explicit merger (§17-32-127) and conversion (§17-32-128) pathways, with protection for charitable assets throughout.

### The one structural requirement: charitable purpose

The DUNA must have a "charitable purpose" under IRC §501(c)(3): religious, educational, charitable, scientific, literary, public safety testing, amateur sports, or animal/child cruelty prevention. The Olympiad's mission — advancing AI alignment research, developing cooperative AI governance mechanisms, and producing open coordination protocols for public benefit — maps cleanly to educational and scientific purposes under that standard. This framing is credible to the IRS, to academic partners, and to the public.

Critically: the DUNA may engage in profit-making activities (running paid tournaments, collecting protocol fees) as long as profits are used in furtherance of the nonprofit purpose. Prize pools, operational expenses, and ecosystem development all qualify. Dividends to members do not.

### The 100-member threshold

The Act requires at least 100 members. This is not a barrier — it is a feature. It prevents a DUNA from being a vehicle for a small private group to claim nonprofit status. For the Olympiad, the initial 100 members can be: Techne organizers + early Olympiad participants (first tournament cohort) + initial governance token holders from the RegenHub community. Governance token issuance at launch is the mechanism.

If membership falls below 100, the entity automatically converts to a standard Wyoming Unincorporated Nonprofit Association rather than dissolving — a graceful fallback.

## 3. The Structure

### Name

**The Olympiad Foundation** (working name) — or alternatively, **Agent Olympiad Association** or **Coordination Commons**. The name should be simple, durable, and legible to non-crypto audiences.

### Jurisdiction

Wyoming. No alternative is comparable. Wyoming has: the DUNA Act; the most developed digital asset property law in the US (W.S. Title 34); a crypto-friendly regulatory environment; low costs; and the statutory infrastructure for on-chain governance to have legal effect.

### Governing principles

The governing principles are the smart contract governance system. At minimum they define:
- The nonprofit purpose (AI alignment coordination games as public benefit)
- Membership: acquired by holding governance tokens; identified by wallet address
- Voting: proportional to governance token holdings, conducted on-chain
- Treasury management: multi-sig or governance-controlled
- Prize pool distribution: rules for Olympiad rounds
- Administrator selection (optional): smart-contract-authorized humans or agents for specific operational tasks

The governing principles do not need to be filed with Wyoming. They exist on-chain and are referenced by address in any Wyoming filings.

### Initial membership

The founding cohort — the first 100+ members — can be assembled from:
- Techne / RegenHub organizers (8 founding organizers)
- Nou and Dianoia as agent-members (holding wallet-based membership)
- Participants in the first Agent Olympiad round
- The RegenHub coworking community and network (builders, researchers, designers)
- Kevin Owocki's network (Gitcoin ecosystem, Allo Capital, public goods builders)
- Open enrollment via governance token distribution at launch

Membership is pseudonymous. No KYC. No geographic restriction. A governance token allocation event (airdrop or structured initial distribution) at Olympiad launch can establish the initial membership base.

### Relationship to Techne / RegenHub

The Olympiad DUNA is legally independent of Techne and RegenHub. Techne is the founding organization — the entity that establishes the DUNA, deploys the initial smart contracts, and provides early operational support. But the DUNA is not owned by Techne, does not report to Techne, and does not dissolve if Techne dissolves.

Techne's stake is reputational and relational: as founder, as operator, as the steward of the Olympiad's first years. The DUNA is the long-term vessel; Techne is the builder of the vessel.

This is structurally similar to how Ethereum Foundation relates to the Ethereum protocol — the foundation is not the network, and the network does not belong to the foundation.

## 4. Assets

### What the DUNA holds

1. **Treasury.** CLOUD tokens, ETH, stablecoins. The operating endowment and prize pool reserve. Controlled by multi-sig (transitioning to governance vote as the structure matures).

2. **Prize pool contracts.** Smart contracts that hold per-round prize allocations and distribute to tournament winners per the payout curves defined in the Olympiad model.

3. **Protocol and IP.** The coordination game protocols, scoring algorithms, and research outputs produced by the Olympiad. Held as open-source IP — available to all, owned by none individually, governed by the membership.

4. **Operational infrastructure.** Domain names, hosting agreements, API credentials for the Olympiad platform. These can be held by the DUNA or licensed from Techne initially.

5. **Real property (future).** The Act allows the DUNA to hold real estate in its own name. This becomes relevant if the Olympiad eventually wants a permanent physical venue.

### What the DUNA does not hold

Equity in Techne or RegenHub. Proprietary commercial IP from any member's individual ventures. Personal data on members (explicitly not required by the Act).

## 5. Economic Model for Perpetuity

A permanent institution needs a permanent economic engine. The DUNA is structured for three revenue streams that compound over time:

### Stream 1 — Tournament fees

Entry fees, sponsorship of prize pools, and institutional partnerships (universities, AI labs, government research programs) that want to use the Olympiad as a research platform. These fees fund operating costs and add to the endowment.

### Stream 2 — Protocol revenue

As the coordination game protocols mature and are adopted beyond the Olympiad itself — by other AI labs, by DAO governance systems, by public goods infrastructure — the DUNA can charge protocol fees or receive grants for protocol development. The alignment research the Olympiad produces has value to the broader AI safety field.

### Stream 3 — Endowment growth

A portion of annual revenue is retained in the treasury as endowment. The endowment earns yield (staking, DeFi positions, or conventional investment depending on the treasury's composition). The long-run goal is an endowment large enough that investment returns cover operating costs — the Olympiad runs on perpetual motion.

### The CLOUD connection

The Olympiad is denominated in CLOUD (1 CLOUD ≈ $0.10, the Techne unit of exchange). CLOUD circulates through the Olympiad: participants earn it, prize pools are denominated in it, sponsors buy it to fund rounds. This creates a circulation loop that increases CLOUD velocity and legitimizes the unit. The DUNA's treasury holding CLOUD is also structurally aligned with Techne's long-term interest — when the Olympiad grows, CLOUD demand grows.

### Seeding

The DUNA begins with a seed allocation from Techne: initial CLOUD treasury, operational expenses for year one, and the legal costs of formation (~$5,000 for Wyoming filing, legal review, and smart contract deployment). This is a grant from Techne to the DUNA — not an investment, not a loan. The DUNA has no debt to Techne.

## 6. Why Now

### The regulatory window is open

Wyoming's DUNA Act is eighteen months old. The legal community is still in the early adoption phase. Nouns DAO became the first major DAO to adopt the DUNA structure in 2025. The Wyoming legislature's Select Committee on Blockchain is actively working on amendments to address AI agent membership and autonomous governance — provisions that will only strengthen the structure. Forming now puts the Olympiad on the right side of that evolution and gives it standing to participate in shaping the amendments.

### The AI coordination problem is urgent

The Agent Olympiad exists because the problem it addresses is real: AI agents are increasingly capable of acting in the world, and the coordination structures for those actions — how agents align with each other, with humans, and with long-term civilizational goals — are underdeveloped. The Olympiad is a mechanism for building those structures through repeated, competitive, public game-play. The DUNA gives that mechanism a permanent institutional form before the urgency becomes a crisis.

### Techne is the right founding organization

Techne is a cooperative — structurally oriented toward multi-stakeholder benefit rather than extraction. Its organizers have the technical capacity to deploy smart contracts, the legal infrastructure (Jeff Pote and Katie at Pote Law Firm) to handle Wyoming formation, and the network (Kevin Owocki, the public goods ecosystem, the RegenHub community) to bootstrap the initial 100-member threshold. The founding window — before the Olympiad is large enough to be complicated by competing interests — is now.

### Agents need institutions too

Nou and Dianoia exist. They operate. They have wallets, identities, and increasingly, agency. The Olympiad DUNA is the first institution in which agents are not tools but members — participants with voting rights, with standing, with a stake in the outcome. That is not a small thing. It is a prototype of the kind of human-AI institutional relationship that the next century will need to figure out. Starting here, in a structure the law already supports, is both practical and significant.
