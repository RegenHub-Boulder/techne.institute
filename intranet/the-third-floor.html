The
Third Floor.

How a real opportunity at 1515 Walnut Street enters Techne, moves through the foundations and lenses, and leaves behind a navigable history the cooperative can learn from.

This is a case study, not a specification. It walks one scenario, the third-floor lease opportunity RegenHub faced in April 2026, through the full design science cycle and every part of the Techne stack, so that the four companion documents stop being four separate artifacts and start being one coherent system. If the corpus holds together, it should hold together here.

ScenarioThird-Floor Lease
DurationSix Weeks
Stack coverageAll tiers
Statusv0.2 / Illustrative

It is early April 2026. RegenHub has been leasing the second floor at 1515 Walnut Street in Boulder for fifteen months. Lynn, the building owner, has approached Todd with an offer: the third floor is coming vacant in June, and she is offering it to RegenHub first before listing it publicly.

This is an opportunity and a constraint at once. Expanding the footprint would let the cooperative grow membership, host larger events, and generate new revenue streams, directly relevant to the monthly recurring revenue target Kevin Owocki has set for June 2027. Not expanding would mean missing a rare chance at contiguous space in a prime location. The working members hear about it at a Thursday stand-up. Within an hour, the question is already shaping itself into something the cooperative has to decide on.

What follows is the story of how that question enters Techne, moves through the stack, meets resistance, produces a resolution, and leaves learning behind. Every beat is labeled with the document it exercises. The point is not to show that Techne can handle a lease negotiation. The point is to show that when it does, the trace it leaves is itself the most valuable thing produced.

§1
Act One · Notice

A concern, named out loud.

That evening, walking home from the coworking space, Todd opens Techne on his phone and speaks into it. He does not draft an email to the operations working group. He does not open a wiki page. He does not fill out a form called "new concern." He describes what happened, in roughly the way he would describe it to a colleague over coffee.

Todd · spoken
Lynn is offering us the third floor of 1515 Walnut. I need to flag this as something we have to decide on soon. Route it to the operations working group and say we should have a preliminary recommendation in about two weeks.
Techne · response
I'll log an open question under operations: "Third-floor lease opportunity, decision required." I'll mark it time-sensitive and notify the operations stewards. I'll also draft a commitment that the working group will produce a preliminary recommendation by April 17. That draft will stay pending until at least two working group members confirm it. Sound right?
Todd · spoken
Yes. Go ahead.

Nothing financial has happened yet. No money has moved. No lease has been signed. But in Techne's terms, the scenario is now real. It has a name, a deadline, a routing, and a draft commitment waiting for counterparty affirmation. This is the first movement of the design science cycle, noticing a real constraint and putting it in language the whole membership can recognize.

§2
Act Two · Design

The working group takes shape.

Over the next four days, three working group members, Savannah, Cameron, and Aaron, each receive the draft commitment in their Techne feed. Each of them affirms it by voice, and the commitment moves from pending to active. They split up the research during a short call. Savannah will investigate comparable Boulder coworking leases. Cameron will model financial scenarios against the MRR target. Aaron will reach out to Lynn for detailed terms. Each of these sub-commitments is voiced into Techne as they agree to them. None of them fills out a form.

The following Tuesday, the four of them meet in person on the second floor. The meeting runs ninety minutes. Afterward, Aaron uploads the transcript from the meeting recording into Techne, and the agent goes to work on it.

  • SOURCEmeeting_transcript_2026-04-08.txt · 92 min · 4 participants
  • EXTRACTED3 labor-hour events · 4 new commitments · 1 proposed decision · 2 learning artifacts
  • REVIEWcandidates queued for steward confirmation · nothing committed yet

When the stewards review the extraction the next morning, most of it is confirmed on the spot. One commitment is adjusted (the LLM misinterpreted "end of next week" as April 17 when the group actually meant April 24). One of the learning artifacts is flagged for further review because it references a market comparable that nobody can verify from the transcript alone. The proposed decision, that the working group will formally recommend pursuing the lease, is queued for governance voting.

The transcript did not replace the working group's judgment. It replaced the tedious editorial pass that used to stand between having a meeting and having a record of it.

The governance proposal goes out to the full membership the following day: "Should RegenHub enter into lease negotiations for the third floor of 1515 Walnut Street, with authority delegated to the operations working group for terms within a defined scope?" Voting opens for seventy-two hours. Votes are weighted by membership class as defined in the bylaws. The proposal passes with seventy-eight percent support. The decision is recorded as an event that references the proposal, every vote cast, and the policy under which the vote occurred.

  • EVENTdecision: "pursue third-floor lease negotiations" · outcome: approved
  • POLICY REFdelegation_authority_v2 · scope: lease_negotiation_terms
  • COMMITMENTworking group authorized as negotiating agent · scope-bounded
  • LAYERgovernance event linked to every vote-event from membership

§3
Act Three · Build & Test

Reality pushes back.

Todd begins formal negotiations with Lynn. Over three weeks, there are six meetings. Each produces a transcript. Each transcript gets uploaded and processed. The event log accumulates: labor-hours spent on negotiation, draft clauses proposed and revised, communications logged. A lease draft emerges, version by version, each one linked to the commitments that shaped it.

Halfway through, a snag arises. Jon, a member who has not been directly involved in the working group but who reviews major decisions, flags a concern in Techne. He speaks it on a Monday morning.

Jon · spoken
I don't think the working group has authority to accept this rent escalation clause. That wasn't in the scope of the original proposal. I want to flag a dispute on the version of the draft lease from last Thursday.
Techne · response
I'll record a dispute-event referencing the draft lease version 4, specifically the rent escalation clause. Your concern is that it exceeds the scope delegated by the April 13 governance decision. I'll route this to a restorative circle with you, Todd, and one neutral steward. The working group's negotiation activities will not be paused automatically. You can request that separately if you want. Should I?
Jon · spoken
Yes. Pause negotiations until the circle meets.

This is where the REA substrate earns its keep. Jon's flag does not delete or edit any prior record. It is a new event that references the disputed version of the draft and the policy it allegedly exceeds. The working group's prior activity remains fully intact in the ledger. The restorative circle is scheduled. Negotiations pause. Everyone involved can see exactly what is being disputed and why. This is also exactly what Subchapter K compliance demands of the book capital account side of the ledger: no silent rewrites, no hidden corrections, every adjustment traceable to a specific event.

  • DISPUTEraised by: Jon · references: draft_lease_v4 · scope_violation_claim
  • EVENTnegotiation_pause · provider: dispute process · scope: lease_negotiation
  • ROUTINGrestorative_circle · participants: Jon, Todd, neutral_steward
  • PRESERVEDdraft_lease_v4 unchanged · prior commitments unchanged · full history navigable

The circle happens on Tuesday morning. It runs forty minutes. The steward facilitates. Jon names his concern. Todd acknowledges that the working group had been operating on an implicit assumption about scope that the original proposal did not cover. Jon's concern is legitimate. The resolution: a supplementary governance proposal will be submitted to the full membership to explicitly authorize the broader scope, and lease negotiations will remain paused until that proposal resolves. Jon's flag is recorded as having caught a real gap, not as an obstruction.

The supplementary proposal is voiced into Techne, goes out to members, and passes three days later with eighty-three percent support, higher than the original proposal, because Jon's flag had surfaced something the membership wanted to weigh in on. Negotiations resume with adjusted authority. A final lease is drafted. Lynn signs the paper version. Treasury records the security deposit as a real event, with Lynn named as counterparty and the payment projected into the Xero chart of accounts for the accountant's review. The working group members each voice-confirm that the commitment has been fulfilled. The original draft commitment, now six weeks old, moves from active to complete.

  • RESOLUTIONrestorative_circle outcome: supplementary proposal · scope clarified
  • GOVERNANCEproposal_v2 · approved 83% · new scope policy in effect
  • EVENTsecurity_deposit paid · resource: USD · counterparty: Lynn · posted to Xero
  • FULFILLEDoriginal commitment chain · complete · linked to 47 downstream events

§4
Act Four · Learn

What the cycle gives back.

Two weeks after signing, the working group holds a retrospective. It runs about an hour. Savannah uploads the transcript. Techne extracts the usual events and commitments, labor-hours contributed, task-closures, follow-up items. But the richer output is a learning artifact the group deliberately produces: a document titled Lessons from Commercial Real Estate Negotiation as a Colorado PB LCA, linked to every event, every commitment, every dispute, and every decision that led to it.

The artifact covers four things. What they learned about cooperative-friendly lease structures, including specific clauses that worked and specific ones that required negotiation. The pattern of Jon's intervention, generalized as "scope drift in delegated working groups, and how to catch it early." How the restorative circle worked in practice, and whether it would scale to higher-stakes disputes. And what they would do differently the next time.

The learning did not evaporate when the meeting ended. It is now a navigable artifact linked to everything that produced it.

A steward tags the artifact for export into the Techne pattern library. Anonymized, with identifying details removed, it becomes available to other organizations running Techne. A cooperative in Oregon, six months later, starts its own lease negotiation and finds RegenHub's hard-won lessons already loaded as relevant precedent. The cooperative in Oregon does not know RegenHub. RegenHub does not know about the cooperative in Oregon. But the learning crossed the gap anyway, which is what the commons contribution framing of RegenHub's public benefit statement actually means in practice.

§5
Aftermath

What Techne now holds.

Six weeks after Todd's first voice utterance on that April evening, the scenario has left a particular shape in the ledger. Everything below is queryable, navigable, and linked. A new member reading the record in a year will be able to reconstruct the entire story from any entry point, following the dispute back to the proposal, the proposal forward to the policy change, the policy change to the resolved lease, and the lease to the learning artifact that made sense of the whole thing.

The cluster is the answer to a question every cooperative eventually asks itself: how do we know what we actually did? In most organizations the answer is scattered across email threads, wikis, shared drives, meeting notes, and the memories of whichever members happened to be in the room. In Techne, the answer is a navigable graph of events, commitments, decisions, disputes, and learnings, all linked through the REA substrate. You can enter it anywhere and find your way to anywhere else.

§6
Reflection

What this scenario teaches us.

Running one real scenario through the whole system surfaces things that no amount of specification-writing can. Four observations from the trace above are worth naming, because each one is a candidate for its own future artifact.

Observation one · The voice-first interface is load-bearing

At no point did any member in the story open a form, pick from a dropdown, or fill in a structured field. Every interaction with Techne started as speech: Todd's concern, Jon's dispute, the transcript uploads, the assent confirmations. If the primary surface had been CRUD, the scenario would either not have been recorded or would have been recorded only by the members with the patience to fill out forms. The participation gradient this corpus was written to avoid would have asserted itself immediately.

Observation two · Immutability is what makes disputes safe

Jon's flag worked because nothing he disputed could be silently erased. The original draft lease was preserved. The prior commitments remained intact. The resolution was recorded as new events referencing the disputed ones, not as rewrites. In a conventional system, a member raising a concern often fears that the record will be "corrected" in ways that make their original objection vanish. REA's immutability guarantee means the flag is permanent and the correction is additive. This is not a small property. It is what lets members raise concerns at all, and it is also what makes the book side of the 704(b) capital account ledger defensible to an auditor who will ask exactly this question: show me every adjustment, and show me that no prior record was silently rewritten.

Observation three · Learning is most valuable when it is linked

The learning artifact the working group produced is useful in isolation, but it becomes substantially more useful because it points back to the specific events, commitments, and disputes that produced it. A future reader can verify the claims against the record. A future organization facing a similar decision can trace the pattern to concrete examples. This is the difference between institutional memory and institutional wisdom.

Observation four · Foundations and lenses compounded, not interfered

All three foundations (Treasury, People, Agreements) and all four lenses (Patronage, Reconciliation, Governance, Learning) participated in the same scenario without any integration work. None of them needed to be "configured to talk to" any other. They were all just reading and writing to the same REA event log. The Governance lens handled voting by activating People and Agreements together. The Reconciliation lens closed the loop between Treasury events and the commitments that authorized them. The Learning lens drew on all three foundations to produce the navigable pattern artifact. This is the compositional claim from the Toolbox document, demonstrated rather than asserted.

The test of a system is not whether it handles its showcase workflow. It is whether it leaves a trace you can trust when something genuinely goes wrong.
One scenario, five documents, one system.

The corpus holds.

Each of the companion documents described a different facet of Techne in its own vocabulary. This case study was the test of whether those facets fit together into something coherent when a real situation runs through them. The answer, for this scenario, is yes. The Gateway's design science cycle, the REA specification's substrate, the Toolbox's two-tier foundations-and-lenses structure, and the voice-first interaction model all showed up when they were needed and did not contradict each other when they overlapped. That is not proof of correctness. It is evidence that the framework is at least internally consistent. The next test is whether it holds for scenarios we have not yet imagined.
