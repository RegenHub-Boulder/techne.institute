The interface is a conversation.

Speak
the Ledger.

How Techne is actually used, and why Treasury needs a microphone before it needs a form.

This document specifies the primary human-computer interaction model for every foundation and lens in the Techne stack, using Treasury as the worked example. Members use Techne by speaking to it, either in real time or by uploading a transcript, and a language model translates what they said into the formal REA operations that live underneath. A conventional interface still exists for power users, edge cases, and audit. It is not the front door.

§1

The interface is the gatekeeper.

Every cooperative we know of has the same hidden problem. The technically confident members do most of the recording. The others do not. It is not because the others do not want to. It is because the interface asks them to learn a data model, memorize a vocabulary, and press a button that commits a permanent record. That is a lot to ask of someone who just wants to note that they contributed four hours this morning.

The result is a participation gradient baked into the tooling itself. The people who can speak the system's language shape what the system remembers. Everyone else becomes invisible to it. For an organization whose legitimacy rests on member participation and patronage accounting, especially one running under Subchapter K where 704(b) allocations depend on a clean record of who contributed what, that is a structural problem, not a usability complaint.

Traditional enterprise software solved this by hiring more analysts and running more training sessions. Cooperatives cannot afford that and should not want to. What we can do, now, in a way we could not do three years ago, is remove the translation burden from the member and place it on a language model. Members describe what happened in the way they would describe it to another member. The system figures out the rest, proposes a record, and asks for confirmation. No form to learn. No vocabulary to memorize. No button whose consequences are unclear.

Every member already knows how to use Techne. They already know how to talk.
§2

The voice-first interaction loop.

The interaction model is a round trip with four movements: the member speaks (or uploads a recording), the agent interprets, the system proposes a formal record, and the member confirms or corrects. Every part of this loop is designed so that trust accumulates rather than being demanded up front.

Two properties of this loop matter more than the rest. First, the system's proposal back to the member is in the member's own register, not in the formal vocabulary of the REA substrate. The member said "four hours on grants." The system replies "four labor-hours to the grant working group." Recognizable, but with enough precision that the member can catch a misinterpretation. Second, no event is written to the ledger until the member confirms. The agent can be wrong. The member is the ground truth. This is the opposite of a traditional form interface, where hitting "save" is a commitment to whatever you typed, correct or not.

Architecture. Client captures audio, sends to a transcription service (Whisper or equivalent), the transcription plus conversation context is sent to an LLM with a system prompt containing the Techne tool schema, the LLM returns a proposed tool call (treasury.record_event, treasury.query_balance, agreements.propose_commitment, etc.), the proposed call is serialized into a natural-language confirmation, and the confirmation is spoken or displayed back to the member. Only on explicit affirmation does the tool call execute against the REA substrate.
§3

A worked session: Treasury through voice.

Here is what Treasury actually looks like in use. Savannah is walking home from the RegenHub coworking space on a Thursday evening, talking into her phone. She is not using a form. She is not looking at a dashboard. She is telling Techne what happened.

Scene one · Recording a contribution

Savannah · spoken
I spent about four hours this afternoon working on the Climate Arts grant with Cameron. Maybe four and a half if you count the call at the end.
Techne · response
I'll record four and a half labor-hours from you to the Climate Arts grant working group, on Thursday April 12th. Cameron is named as a co-participant. Does that sound right, or would you like to adjust the hours or the attribution?
Savannah · spoken
Yes, that's right. Go ahead.
Techne · response
Recorded. Your patronage balance with Climate Arts is now twelve and a half hours this cycle. Anything else?

Notice what did not happen. Savannah did not open a form. She did not pick a project from a dropdown. She did not fill in a duration in minutes. She did not look up Cameron in a member directory. The system did all of that for her, proposed the structured record in language she could verify, and only wrote it when she said yes. The whole exchange took under thirty seconds.

Scene two · Checking a balance

Savannah · spoken
How much have I contributed total this quarter?
Techne · response
Across all working groups this quarter, you've contributed forty-seven labor-hours, split mostly between Climate Arts (twelve and a half), the third-floor lease negotiation (eighteen), and commons stewardship (sixteen). Want me to break any of those down, or show how they translate into patronage credits?
Savannah · spoken
No, that's good. Thanks.

The read-back is shaped by what the system knows about Savannah and her typical patterns, not a generic balance printout but something closer to what a colleague would tell her if she asked the same question in the hallway. The underlying query is still a formal aggregation over the REA event log. The member just never sees it.

Scene three · Generating a report for the accountant

Savannah · spoken
Can you pull together the quarterly financials for the accountant? She needs the conventional view, with cash accounts only.
Techne · response
I'll generate a Q1 2026 financial statement covering January through March, projected from the REA event log into the Xero chart of accounts and filtered to monetary resource types only. That will exclude labor-hours, patronage credits, and in-kind contributions, which is what an accountant usually wants. I'll include the 704(b) book capital account summary separately for the Subchapter K allocation work. Ready in about thirty seconds. Where should I send it?

This is where the voice-first model meets the coexistence framing from the REA specification. Savannah's request is in plain language. The system translates it into a projection over the same event log that holds everything else. The accountant gets a conventional financial statement plus the 704(b) view she actually needs. Savannah never had to know what "filtered to monetary resource types only" means, even though that is exactly what the system did.

§4

The async path: transcript ingestion.

Voice in real time is one path into Techne. The other is asynchronous. A working group meets, produces a transcript (automatically generated by the meeting tool, or recorded and transcribed later), and uploads it. The agent reads the transcript, extracts proposed events, commitments, decisions, and learnings, and surfaces them for a steward to review before anything is committed.

This path matters especially for RegenHub because we already live this workflow. Transcripts from meetings with Kevin, Aaron, Cameron, Yev, and others have been edited and synthesized by hand for months. What Techne does is turn a labor-intensive, error-prone editorial pass into a structured extraction that still ends in human review, but that no longer depends on someone having the time and patience to do it manually. The transcript remains the source of truth. Stewards still decide what to commit. The system just does the tedious extraction in between.

The same path handles transcripts a member uploads directly: a recording from a partner call, a voice memo describing what happened at a community event, notes from a one-on-one. Any linear transcript of speech becomes a source that can seed records across both tiers of the toolbox.

§5

Vocabulary resolution, not glossary memorization.

The companion documents introduce serious vocabulary: agent, resource, event, commitment, duality, patronage, stockflow, substrate, foundation, lens. A living glossary is useful for members who want to go deep. But the voice-first model makes most of that vocabulary optional for daily use, because the system translates between the member's everyday language and the formal model.

What follows is a sample of how that translation happens in practice. The left column is what a member might actually say. The right column is what the system parses it into. Members are never required to speak in the right column's register, but they can, and power users will.

"I spent four hours on grants this morning."

project: grants working group
"The coop paid Jess for her legal review."
→

"I'll pick up the third-floor walkthrough next week."

due: next week

"We decided to move the treasury to Mercury."
→

"How much have I put in this quarter?"

"I don't think Cameron ever got credited for the permit work."

The translation layer doubles as a living glossary in reverse. Instead of asking members to learn what "stockflow" means, Techne learns what the members in this organization actually say when they mean it. Over time the translation table becomes a record of the cooperative's vernacular, which is itself a valuable artifact, because it captures how the membership actually talks about its own work.

Vocabulary resolution. A per-organization synonym map maintained alongside the type registry. When the LLM parses an utterance and encounters an unfamiliar term, it flags the term as a synonym_candidate and routes it through a steward for confirmation. Confirmed synonyms are added to the map and surfaced in future prompts, so the system gets better at each cooperative's dialect over time without anyone having to curate a glossary by hand.
§6

Confirmation, correction, and earned trust.

A voice-first system that wrote records without confirmation would be worse than useless. It would quietly corrupt the ledger and erode trust in the whole substrate. Trust in Techne is earned through three disciplines that apply to every voice interaction.

Discipline one · Read-back before commit

Every proposed record is read back in plain language before it is written. The member has to confirm. If they do not confirm, nothing happens. This is the simplest form of consent in the interaction model, and it is non-negotiable.

Discipline two · Corrections are events, not edits

When a member says "actually, it was three hours, not four," the system does not rewrite the original event. It records a correction event that references the original, and derived balances update accordingly. The audit trail is preserved. The original utterance is preserved. The correction is preserved. Nothing is silently overwritten, which is one of the core commitments of the REA substrate and a requirement for maintaining clean 704(b) book capital accounts.

Discipline three · Confidence scoring and graceful uncertainty

The LLM returns a confidence score with every proposed interpretation. Below a threshold, the system does not guess. It asks. "I heard you mention a working group, but I'm not sure which one. Was it Climate Arts or the third-floor lease?" This is better behavior than a form can offer, because a form cannot tell you it is not sure.

Trust is not assumed. It is earned one confirmation at a time, and it survives because corrections never rewrite history.
§7

The conventional interface is the escape hatch.

A CRUD interface still exists, and it is important. Some tasks are not well served by voice: bulk reconciliation, audit reviews, policy drafting sessions, anything involving dense tabular data or comparison across many records. Some members will simply prefer typing. Some situations require a visual overview that voice cannot provide. And some things (the annual report, the external audit, the K-1 package, the tax filing) must be reviewed in a traditional format before they leave the building.

Both surfaces read from and write to the same REA event log. A record created by voice shows up in the GUI immediately. A correction made in the GUI is visible to the next voice query. There is no sync, no reconciliation, no drift, because there is only ever one source of truth underneath, and both surfaces are just different ways of speaking to it.

§8

Implementation notes for builders.

What follows is the minimum technical picture needed to start building this. Full technical architecture will land in a companion document. This section is the shape of the thing.

Transcription. Whisper (or equivalent) running either client-side for privacy-sensitive contexts or via API. Output: text plus word-level timestamps plus confidence per segment.
Intent parsing. LLM call (Claude is the reference implementation; others supported behind a swappable interface) with a system prompt containing (a) the REA type catalog for this organization, (b) the per-organization synonym map, (c) the member's recent conversation context, and (d) the tool schema for every foundation and lens the organization has adopted. Output: a structured tool call, a confidence score, and a natural-language confirmation string.
Tool schema. Each foundation and lens exposes a small set of parameterized operations under its own namespace: treasury.record_event, treasury.query_balance, people.register_agent, agreements.propose_commitment, governance.submit_proposal, patronage.query_balance, learning.capture_artifact. The LLM is told which operations exist and what arguments they take. New foundations or lenses plug in by registering their schema with the intent parser.
Stack alignment. The reference stack is Glide as member-facing surface, Supabase for the REA substrate, Make.com for automation, Claude API for intent parsing. Hosted clients run this stack; self-hosted clients substitute components that implement the same interfaces.
Confirmation gate. No tool call executes until the member affirms. Affirmation can be spoken ("yes," "go ahead," "record it") or tapped. A negative response routes back to a clarification prompt rather than discarding context.
Audit trail. Every voice interaction is logged as its own meta-event: the original utterance, the transcription, the parsed intent, the proposed tool call, the confirmation or correction, and the final committed operation. This log is inspectable by the member and by stewards, and is itself subject to REA immutability.
§9

User stories, voice-first.

Treasury · voice-first requirements
  • As a member walking home from a working session, I want to record my contribution by speaking into my phone, so that the friction of logging is low enough that I actually do it.
  • As a member, I want the system to read back what it thinks I said before it records anything, so I can catch misinterpretations before they become permanent.
  • As a member, I want to correct a record by speaking the correction, without having to navigate to the original entry and edit it.
  • As a member, I want to ask about my contribution or balance in plain language, and receive an answer that is also in plain language.
  • As a steward, I want to upload a meeting transcript and receive a set of candidate events, commitments, decisions, and learnings I can review before any of them are committed.
  • As a member who prefers typing, I want the same capabilities available through a text input as through voice, with identical behavior.
  • As a member auditing a record, I want to see the full history of the voice interaction that produced it: the utterance, the interpretation, the confirmation.
  • As a steward, I want to review and confirm new vocabulary that the system has encountered, so the per-organization synonym map grows deliberately rather than by guess.
§10

What this costs.

Voice-first is the right bet, and it has real costs that deserve to be named rather than hidden in marketing language.

Dependence on language models. This design assumes an LLM good enough to parse intent reliably. That was not possible three years ago and may not be equally available forever. The mitigation is that the REA substrate is independent of the interface layer. If the LLM becomes unavailable or unaffordable, the GUI escape hatch still works, and no data is lost.

Privacy. Voice data is more sensitive than form data. Members must have clear choices about whether transcription happens locally or via API, whether recordings are retained, and who can access the interaction log. These are governance questions as much as technical ones and belong in the Governance lens, not hard-coded here.

Misinterpretation risk. LLMs can be wrong in confident ways. The read-back discipline catches most errors, but not all. The audit trail means corrections are always possible, but a member who stops paying attention to confirmations can still let bad records slip through. We will need to watch this empirically.

Accessibility asymmetry. Voice works well for some members and poorly for others. Members who are non-native speakers of the system's language, members with speech differences, members in noisy environments, and members who simply prefer text all need first-class support. The answer is that the text input path must be exactly as capable as the voice path, and that is a design requirement, not a fallback.

The goal is not voice for its own sake. The goal is removing the translation burden from members. Whatever achieves that is the interface.

The ledger should listen.

For a century, cooperative software has asked its members to learn the software before they could be counted in the record. Voice-first inverts that. The member speaks in their own language, to their own device, and the system does the translation. The ledger learns to hear them, rather than the other way around. That is what every foundation and lens in Techne is built for, and Treasury is only the first place we are proving it.
