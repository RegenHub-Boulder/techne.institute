01 Orientation

What is design science, and why does it belong here?

Design science is the disciplined practice of making things that work. Where the natural sciences describe the world as it is, design science addresses the world as it ought to be: problems, interventions, and the patient iteration between them.

Its lineage runs through Herbert Simon's Sciences of the Artificial, Christopher Alexander's pattern languages, Douglas Engelbart's augmentation research, and the information systems traditions that followed. What unites them is a conviction: building something, watching it behave in the world, and learning from it is a legitimate way of knowing. Often it is the only way, when the questions are about human systems.

For a cooperative, this matters. We are not inheriting a fixed organization and administering it. We are designing one, continuously, together. Design science gives us shared language for doing that deliberately rather than by accident. It also connects our practice to a much older inheritance, one that stretches from tally sticks through Lovelace, Bush, Wiener, Licklider, and Engelbart: the long project of augmenting human collective intelligence with tools we build and refine in common.

The artifact is itself a way of knowing. We learn what our cooperative is by building it, testing it, and refining what we built.
02 Your Path Into the Studio

From governance documents to lived practice.

This document sits between what you have just read and what comes next. The articles, bylaws, and member agreement oriented you to our legal form, our commitments, and our public benefit. The Techne studio is where those commitments become daily work. Design science is the bridge.

03 The Practice

A cycle we walk together.

Design science is not a procedure to follow mechanically. It is a disciplined rhythm that any RegenHub initiative can participate in, whether that is a working group, a tool build, a programming experiment, or a governance revision. The four movements below are the minimum grammar.

  1. Notice a real constraint

    Begin with something that actually pinches. A friction, a gap, a commitment we are failing to keep. Name it in language the whole membership can recognize.

  2. Design an intervention

    Draw on theory, precedent, and member wisdom. Make your design rationale explicit: what we chose, what we rejected, what we are assuming. Rationale is an artifact too.

  3. Build and test with real stakeholders

    An untested design is a hypothesis, not a solution. Ship small. Invite the people affected into the test. Measure against our public benefit commitments, not just function.

  4. Learn, and share what you learned

    Capture what worked, what broke, and why. Feed it back into the studio so the next member, or the next cooperative, does not start from zero. Learning withheld is commons enclosed.

04 What You Will Meet Next

The Techne stack: an operating grammar for cooperation.

Inside the studio, our tools are organized as three layers drawn from the REA accounting ontology: Resources, Events, Agents. In plainer language, we call these Treasury, Agreements, and People. Each layer answers a distinct cooperative question, and together they compose the operational grammar of RegenHub.

Treasury is how we give, save, and invest together. Agreements is how we name what we promise one another and record what actually happens. People is how we hold membership, contribution, and care. The layers are designed to be interoperable with each other, and eventually to travel: what we refine internally is intended to be offered outward as a modular civic operating system that other cooperatives can adopt in whole or in part.

A phrase we keep returning to: Techne is not an enterprise, it is soil. The stack exists to make the soil more fertile, not to harvest it. Its first job is to keep the collective intelligence of this place, the scenius, legible to every member and transparent to the capital that flows through the system.

05 A Participatory Discipline

Rigor is not the opposite of belonging.

It would be easy to imagine design science as the province of a few technical members with methodology training. That is not how we practice it here. The cycle above works because it is walked together, because the people affected by a design are the same people who help notice the problem, sketch the intervention, test it, and decide whether it earned its keep.

This is what distinguishes our approach from most organizational design literature. We are not running experiments on our members. We are conducting inquiry with them. The rigor we pursue is the rigor of a community that takes its own learning seriously, documents what it finds, and shares it outward as part of our public benefit commitment. Augmentation, not automation, is the orienting word. Tools are here to make us more capable together, not to replace the thinking we owe each other.
