§0

How to read this document

This specification is written for two kinds of reader at once: the member who wants to understand what the stack does and why, and the builder who needs a precise enough picture to make design decisions. Two visual markers run throughout so you can navigate at your own depth.

Callouts marked like this are written in plain language. If you skip everything else, read these. You will still understand the essential claims of the document.
Blocks like this carry technical detail: schemas, constraints, invariants. Non-technical readers can skim past them without losing the thread. Technical readers should treat them as normative. MUST, SHOULD, and MAY are used in the RFC-2119 sense.

The main narrative alternates between the two registers on purpose. A cooperative whose members cannot explain their own systems to each other is a cooperative with a widening gap between governance and practice. This document exists to close that gap.

§1

Why REA, and why now

REA (Resource, Event, Agent) is an accounting ontology introduced by William McCarthy in 1982. Its claim is that the debits and credits we inherited from fifteenth-century Venetian merchants are a specific projection of economic reality, not the reality itself. Underneath the ledger, something simpler is happening: agents do things that affect resources. REA models that directly, and then generates the projections we need on top.

This needs to be said clearly. REA does not replace conventional accounting, and the Techne stack will not ask RegenHub to abandon it. The cooperative still has to file partnership returns, complete K-1 allocations for its members, produce financial statements that banks and grant-makers recognize, and maintain the 704(b) capital account compliance that our Subchapter K election requires. Conventional accounting is the format those institutions speak. REA sits beneath that format as a richer substrate, and the conventional view is one report that can be derived from the REA event log whenever it is needed.

What REA gives us on top of the conventional view is everything the conventional view cannot easily represent: labor contribution, patronage, volunteer care work, commons stewardship, reciprocal non-monetary exchange. These are the substance of cooperative life that debits and credits were never designed to track. Both layers coexist. The accountant still gets the statements they need. Our members also get a true picture of the whole economy they are participating in.

Two lenses on the same reality. Conventional accounting answers "how much money moved, and is it balanced?", the question tax authorities and auditors ask. REA answers "what actually happened, between whom, and with what?", the question a cooperative needs for patronage, governance, and public benefit reporting. Techne keeps both lenses available, and the conventional view is generated automatically from the REA event log.
Implementation note: conventional journal entries are produced as a derived view over the event log, using a mapping from REA event types to the chart of accounts maintained in Xero. Because RegenHub elected Subchapter K taxation, the derivation must also feed 704(b) book capital accounts, which differ from tax capital accounts and drive K-1 allocations. Resource types with no monetary analogue (labor-hours, care contributions, commons stewardship) are excluded from the conventional projection but remain fully first-class in the REA layer. No information is lost. Different reports use different subsets.

Three reasons REA fits RegenHub specifically

First, multi-capital from the start. Our public benefit statement obliges us to track things that have no dollar price: member participation, commons stewardship, knowledge contribution, ecological health. REA lets us model these as first-class resources without pretending they are money.

Second, agents are first-class. In REA, every event names who gave and who received. There is no anonymous ledger. This is exactly the transparency a cooperative needs, and exactly the condition under which patronage accounting under Subchapter K becomes tractable. We can always answer the question a 704(b) audit will ask: what has this member contributed, what have they received, and how does that flow through their capital account?

Third, reciprocity is built in. The duality principle (§3) says every give has a corresponding take. That is not just a bookkeeping rule. It is the mathematical shape of reciprocal exchange. REA encodes the ethic of the commons into the structure of the records themselves.

Conventional accounting tells us what the money did. REA tells us what we did together, and the money view falls out of it whenever we need it.
§2

The core model: Resource · Event · Agent

The model has three primitives. Everything else in the Techne stack is built from them or from relationships between them.

The three primitives

Resource. Anything a community values and wants to track the flow of. Money is a resource. So are labor hours, kilowatt-hours, credits, reputation tokens, square-feet-of-space-for-a-day, a line of code, a cultivated relationship. Resources have a type, a unit of measure, and a current custodian.

Event. A discrete happening that affects one or more resources. Events are the atoms of REA. They are immutable once recorded. You never edit a past event, you record a new one that corrects or counters it. Every event has a timestamp, a type, and references to its participants and affected resources.

Agent. A party who participates in an event. Agents may be individual members, working groups, the cooperative itself, external organizations, or in principle non-human stewards such as a land trust or a software process operating under delegated authority.

In the data model, Event is the central table. It holds foreign keys to at least two Agent rows (the "provider" and "receiver" roles) and at least one Resource row with a signed quantity. Balances are never stored directly. They are always derived by folding over the event log filtered to a given resource and custodian. This is the REA commitment to derivability. The ledger of events is the single source of truth, and on-chain confirmations (when present) verify it without replacing it.
§3

Duality and reciprocity

The single most important rule in REA is the duality principle. Every economic event that decreases a resource for one agent is paired with an event that increases a resource for another. You cannot give without taking, and you cannot take without giving.

When Savannah works four hours on grant writing, two things happen at once. Her labor-hours flow out, and her patronage credits flow in. REA insists on recording both sides. That is what makes the ledger balance, and what makes every contribution visible.

For a cooperative, duality is more than bookkeeping hygiene. It is how we make unpaid and non-monetary contributions legible. A member who contributes four hours of care work is not making a donation into a void. They are entering into a reciprocal relationship with the cooperative that will later translate into patronage allocation, voice in governance, or other forms of mutual benefit. REA forces us to record the other side of that exchange, which means we can never forget it exists. This is also exactly what Subchapter K's "substantial economic effect" standard for 704(b) allocations demands: a paper trail where every economic burden and every economic benefit can be traced to an agent.

Duality is enforced at the data layer. An Event row is not valid until it is bound to a DualityPair that references a counterpart event. Unpaired events MUST either be linked to a standing Contract that names the paired event's type, or be flagged as pending_duality for resolution by a steward.
§4

The three layers

REA is traditionally described in three stacked layers: what is actually happening right now, what has been promised, and what the organization considers permissible in general. The Techne stack uses all three.

Policy layer

This is where the cooperative's rules live. Which event types are allowed? What resources can members exchange with the coop? Who is permitted to receive what? Policies are decisions made by governance and encoded as constraints on the other two layers. In RegenHub, the bylaws and the member agreement are both policy-layer artifacts.

Commitment layer

A commitment is a specific promised future event. "I will contribute ten hours this month" or "the cooperative will pay rent on the first of the month" are commitments. Commitments bundle into contracts, which in REA are not necessarily legal contracts but any coherent set of mutual promises. A member agreement is a contract. A working group charter is a contract. A grant award letter is a contract.

Operational layer

This is the ground truth. When Savannah actually records her four hours, an event is written here. Operational events may fulfill a commitment (closing the loop) or occur freely without prior commitment. Either is allowed.

Think of it this way. The policy layer is what is allowed. The commitment layer is what we said we would do. The operational layer is what we actually did. Techne needs to track all three and show the relationships between them.
§5

The Techne tool layers

Above the REA primitives, the Techne stack exposes three tool layers that members actually interact with: Treasury, People, and Agreements. Each layer is a lens on the shared substrate, not a separate database. They emphasize different REA primitives and different temporal layers, but they all read from and write to the same event log, agent registry, and resource registry.

Treasury

Treasury holds the cooperative's relationship with resources and reports on the three treasury activities at the heart of our identity: giving together, saving together, investing together. It is where balances are read, patronage is computed, and the 704(b) book capital accounts are maintained. It is also where the conventional Xero chart of accounts is kept in sync through derivations from the event log.

People

People holds the agent registry. Membership classes, roles, credentials, and the record of who is in what relationship with the cooperative all live here. This layer is where prospective members apply, where member classes are defined in policy, and where contribution histories become visible as a member's own record. It is the layer through which invisible labor becomes visible.

Agreements

Agreements is the layer of promises and their enactment. A commitment begins here as a proposal, becomes a contract when accepted, generates events as it is fulfilled, and either closes cleanly or enters the dispute process. Governance decisions, when they bind future action, are recorded here. So are grant awards, working group charters, and investor term sheets.

The reason these layers can be adopted independently is that they share the same foundation. An organization could install Treasury alone and still benefit, because Treasury is just REA events over resources. If they later add Agreements, the commitments automatically link to those same events. What we refine internally at RegenHub is intended to be offered outward as a modular civic operating system that other cooperatives, and eventually other organizational forms, can adopt in whole or in part.
§6

Functional requirements

This section states what the Techne stack must do, layer by layer, in the form of user stories. Each story is followed by a technical constraint where one applies. This is the portion of the document that behaves as a PRD. It is what a builder should read before starting work, and what a member should read to confirm that the thing being built reflects the commitments we have made.

Treasury
  • As a member, I want to see every resource the cooperative holds and every resource I personally hold within it, so that nothing is hidden from patronage accounting. MUST: balances derived from event log; never stored as authoritative state.
  • As a steward, I want to record an event that moves a resource between two agents, with both sides captured as a single paired unit. MUST: duality enforced; unpaired events flagged pending_duality.
  • As an auditor, I want every resource change to trace back to an event with two named agents and a timestamp. MUST: events immutable; corrections recorded as new counter-events.
  • As the cooperative, I want a view that maps REA events into the Xero chart of accounts for conventional reporting and K-1 preparation. MUST: conventional projection generated from event log; 704(b) book accounts maintained alongside tax accounts.
People
  • As a prospective member, I want to apply for a specific membership class with its rights and obligations clearly stated. MUST: membership class definitions are policy-layer objects.
  • As a steward, I want to onboard new agents, assign classes, and record credentials as verifiable resources the agent holds. SHOULD: credentials modeled as resources of type credential held by the agent.
  • As a member, I want to see my own contribution history and the standing it gives me, with nothing material hidden behind an aggregate. MUST: member-visible view is queryable by the member without steward mediation.
Agreements
  • As a member, I want to propose a commitment in the form "I will provide X in exchange for Y" and share it with a counterparty. SHOULD: commitments reference resource types, not instances, at draft time.
  • As a counterparty, I want to accept, modify, or decline a proposed commitment, with the history visible. MUST: negotiation history preserved as a sequence of proposal-events.
  • As a member, I want to see my outstanding commitments and which have been fulfilled, with links to the fulfilling events. MUST: commitment fulfillment is a linkage, not a mutation.
  • As a member, I want to flag a disputed event or commitment without deleting it, and route it to an appropriate resolution process. MUST: dispute is a new event referencing the disputed event; original is unchanged; retroactive reversals implemented as forward-dated counter-events.
Cross-cutting: governance & policy
  • As a member, I want to propose a policy that changes the rules for a class of events, for example: "contributions to care work must be credited at parity with technical work." MUST: policies are type-layer objects with an effective date and a version history.
  • As a member, I want to vote on proposals, with my vote weighted according to my membership class. SHOULD: votes are themselves events; voting weight derived from agent class at time of vote.
  • As the cooperative, I want policy changes to be versioned, with any retroactive implications explicitly surfaced for review. MUST: all prior policy versions retained; no silent policy rewrites.
§7

Data model

The following is a minimal, normative data model for the REA substrate that underlies the Techne stack. Implementations may extend it. They may not remove columns marked required, nor replace derivable quantities with stored ones.

Entity summary

Entity Role Key fields
Agent Any party that participates in events id, name, agent_class_id, status
Resource Anything of value whose flow is tracked id, resource_type_id, custodian_id, unit
Event Immutable record of a happening id, type, occurred_at, provider, receiver, resource, quantity
Commitment A specific promised future event id, contract_id, resource_type, due_at, fulfilled_by
Contract A bundle of mutual commitments id, name, effective_from, effective_to, status
Policy Governance rules constraining other entities id, applies_to, rule, effective_from, version
Implementation target is Supabase (Postgres) with row-level security keyed on agent_class, fronted by Glide for member-facing views and orchestrated by Make.com for event automation. Event immutability is enforced by a trigger that rejects UPDATE and DELETE on the events table. Derived views (balances, patronage summaries, commitment status, the Xero projection) are implemented as materialized views refreshed on event insertion. Where on-chain confirmations exist, they are stored in onchain_ref as verification of the off-chain record, never as its authority.
§8

Out of scope & open questions

To keep this specification honest, here is what it does not yet settle. These are open questions the membership needs to work on together.

Out of scope for v0.2

Direct integration with Xero for automatic journal posting (currently a manual reconciliation). Multi-currency conversion rules. Cross-organization event federation. Token issuance and on-chain settlement beyond confirmation of the off-chain record. Full automated treatment of non-monetary patronage allocations under Subchapter K. These are real needs but not yet defined here.

Known gaps in 704(b) coverage

The cooperative's 704(b) compliance work is approximately sixty percent complete, and the data model above needs to be matched by corresponding policy and partnership-agreement language before it can carry the full allocation story. Remaining gaps include the book versus tax capital account distinction, the Qualified Income Offset provision, the book-up revaluation policy for contributed property, and 704(c) tracking for contributed assets with built-in gain or loss. These will be addressed in a companion document coordinated with counsel.

Open questions

How do we handle resource types that defy quantification, such as trust, morale, or ecological health? Do we model them as scalar resources with contested units, or as categorical states? How retroactive can a policy change be before it violates the spirit of event immutability? When a commitment is only partially fulfilled, does the unfulfilled remainder decay, roll forward, or require explicit closure? What does it mean for an outside organization running the Techne stack under white label to fork a policy we have authored, and do we track the lineage? What is the right securities treatment for derivative instruments modeled as resources (royalty units, SAFE notes, compute tokens)?

If you have opinions on any of these, you are not too early and you are not unqualified. These are exactly the questions the design science cycle exists to work through, together, over time.
§9

Glossary

704(b)
The section of the Internal Revenue Code that governs how a partnership allocates income, gain, loss, and deduction among its partners. Because RegenHub elected Subchapter K taxation, 704(b) compliance is the central technical constraint on our allocation and capital account design.
Agent
Any party that can participate in an event: a member, a working group, the cooperative itself, an external organization, or an authorized non-human steward.
Commitment
A specific promised future event, for example "I will contribute ten hours this month." Lives in the commitment layer. May be fulfilled by an operational event, or not.
Contract
A bundle of mutual commitments that hold together as a coherent agreement. In REA, a contract is not necessarily a legal contract. A working group charter qualifies.
Duality
The principle that every give event has a paired take event. What leaves one agent enters another. The ethical shape of reciprocity, expressed in data.
Event
An immutable record of something that happened, naming the agents involved and the resources affected. The atom of REA.
Patronage
In cooperative accounting, the amount a member has contributed or used, used as the basis for allocating surplus. REA makes patronage computable as a fold over a member's events with the cooperative.
Policy
A governance rule that constrains which combinations of agent, resource, and event type are permissible. Lives in the policy layer.
REA
Resource, Event, Agent. An accounting ontology introduced by William McCarthy in 1982. Sits beneath conventional accounting as a richer substrate, from which the debit-credit view can be derived as one report among many.
Resource
Anything of value whose flow a community wants to track. Money, labor hours, square-feet-days of space, a credential, a relationship.
Stockflow
The relationship between an event and the resource it affects. Events are flows. Resources are stocks. Balances are always derived from the stockflow history.
Subchapter K
The partnership taxation regime RegenHub elected, in preference to the default Subchapter T cooperative tax treatment. Subchapter K gives richer allocation flexibility and better fits the multi-capital reality of the cooperative, at the cost of more technical compliance work.
