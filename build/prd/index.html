Scenario engine

A Tool for Estimating the Labor and Infrastructure of Building Digital Things.

The Labor Scenario Engine is a public-facing instrument that translates a proposed digital project into a legible pool of labor hours, role allocations, infrastructure commitments, and pre-authorized pricing. It lives at techne.institute/build and serves two purposes in a single surface: it teaches visitors how web technologies are actually built and maintained, and it generates the pre-authorized invoices that bring work into the cooperative.

01 · Summary

What the engine does.

A visitor arrives at techne.institute/build with a project in mind, whether specific or vague. The engine walks them through a configuration: project identity, pattern selection, scope calibration, infrastructure tier, and ongoing operations window. As the visitor moves, the engine computes a labor allocation across named roles at Fair Market Value rates, an infrastructure estimate across named line items, and a total build-plus-operations cost over the chosen horizon.

When the scenario is settled, the visitor can request a pre-authorized invoice. This is a committed quote from the cooperative's side: a documented offer to perform the work at the priced scope, contingent on member availability and final countersignature. The invoice is paid through Stripe or through Mercury ACH. Every step, from first configuration change through payment receipt, writes a hash to an append-only chain that confirms the authoritative off-chain record.

The engine is not a calculator. It is a working model of how this cooperative thinks about digital labor: as a pool of hours, bound by member capacity and priced at Fair Market Value, organized around a library of patterns that correspond to real systems people build.

02 · Purpose

Why this, and why here.

The cooperative needs a way for prospective customers to arrive at a defensible estimate without first requiring a sales conversation. Sales conversations are expensive in human time and they gate access: only visitors who feel confident enough to pick up the phone ever enter the pipeline. A configuration-first instrument widens the door.

The Institute needs a way to make the cost of digital work visible to students, clients, and the public. Most people who commission digital tools have no working model of where the money goes. They see a quoted number and accept or reject it. The engine shows the underlying structure, which is itself a learning artifact.

The LCA needs a committed front door for incoming work that hands the resulting invoice directly to the financial plumbing already in place: Stripe for card and on-chain payments, Mercury for ACH, Supabase for the authoritative record, a chain attestation layer for public accountability. The engine is designed to fit that plumbing, not compete with it.

03 · Users and surfaces

Who this is for.

Prospective customer

primary
Anyone considering commissioning digital work. The engine gives them a configurable scenario, a defensible estimate, and a path from curiosity to signed engagement without requiring a meeting to begin.

Curious learner

secondary
Visitors using the engine to understand how digital systems are built and maintained. The engine must remain informative even when no invoice is generated. Every number is annotated, every role is explained, every infrastructure choice is described in plain language.

Cooperative member

operator
Members who need visibility into what the engine is committing to on their behalf. Members have a private view showing current capacity commitments, pending pre-authorized invoices, and their own scheduled hours across active engagements.

Operations steward

admin
The staff role that countersigns pre-authorized invoices, confirms member availability, and moves invoices from pending to active. The admin surface is where the engine becomes a commitment rather than a quote.
04 · Functional requirements

What the engine must do.

The requirements below are grouped by function. Each function is a unit of work that can be implemented and tested in isolation. The sequencing of implementation is separate from this document and is held in the development tracker.

4.1 Scenario composition

The visitor composes a scenario through a guided sequence. At any point they can move forward, move back, or reset. The scenario is persisted in the browser session so navigation does not destroy work. Composition is complete when a pattern has been selected and the required calibration fields have values.

4.2 Pattern library

The engine ships with a curated library of patterns, each corresponding to a common digital system. Patterns are stored as structured records with a baseline labor distribution across roles, a baseline infrastructure profile, and a narrative description of what the system actually is. The initial library contains six patterns, expandable by operations staff through the admin surface.

Web applicationAuthenticated, single-tenant. Users have accounts and state. Real ongoing.mid

Data platformIngestion, processing, dashboards. Data pipelines run on schedules.high
Distributed systemMulti-service, possibly multi-modal, with AI inference or chain settlement.high

4.3 Labor taxonomy and FMV rates

The engine holds a role taxonomy. Each role has a Fair Market Value hourly rate calibrated against the regional market for senior practitioners. Rates are versioned; changes are logged; the active rate at the time of pre-authorization is the rate that governs the invoice. The table below is the seed taxonomy for launch.

BackendServer, database, application logic, authentication.$195 / hr

FMV rates are what the customer pays. They are not what the member receives. The member's patronage credit for the work is a separate computation, governed by the cooperative's allocation formula (labor 40, revenue generation 30, cash 20, community 10) and resolved at the capital account layer. The engine does not expose this translation to the customer.

4.4 Member availability reflection

When a scenario is configured, the engine queries the current capacity roster to determine whether the required labor can be fulfilled within the customer's preferred window. Three outcomes are possible and the engine must surface them honestly.

  1. Available. The required hours are fully covered by members currently open to taking work in the requested domain. The engine displays a green confirmation and proceeds to pre-authorization.
  2. Partially available. Some but not all of the required hours are covered. The engine offers three paths: extend the timeline, reduce the scope, or queue the scenario pending capacity.
  3. Not available. The required capacity cannot be matched in the current period. The engine offers to schedule a planning conversation and queue the scenario for a future capacity window.

4.5 Infrastructure cost model

Every pattern carries an infrastructure profile. The profile names the line items a real system of that pattern would carry, estimates the monthly cost for each, and lets the customer toggle between three provisioning tiers. Line items are explicit, not hidden; the customer sees exactly what they are paying to keep the system alive.

TierProvisioning approachTypical range
LeanStatic hosting, managed services at free or low tiers, third-party auth. Suited for low traffic and simple systems.$0 to $75 / mo
StandardManaged PaaS, production database, monitoring, backups, CDN. Suited for real users and real uptime expectations.$75 to $600 / mo
SovereignSelf-hosted on cooperative servers (Boulder node or Hetzner), with control and data locality. Suited for organizations that want to own their infrastructure.$150 to $1,500 / mo

4.6 Cost computation

The build cost is the sum of labor hours multiplied by FMV rates for each role, with a complexity multiplier applied by the customer (range 0.8 to 1.5). The ongoing cost is the sum of monthly infrastructure charges plus a monthly maintenance labor allocation, expressed as a percentage of build labor (default 8 percent). The total engagement cost is the build cost plus the ongoing cost over the customer's selected horizon, typically 6, 12, or 24 months.

4.7 Pre-authorized invoice generation

When the visitor requests a pre-authorized invoice, the engine produces a structured document containing the project identity, the pattern selection, the role-level labor allocation, the infrastructure profile, the monthly ongoing estimate, the total over horizon, a pricing lock window (default 14 days), a member availability confirmation, and an append-only hash referencing the scenario state at authorization time. The invoice is stored in Supabase, surfaced to operations staff for final review, and emailed to the customer as a PDF with a unique hash-linked URL.

4.8 Payment pipeline

On final approval by operations staff, the invoice becomes payable through two rails. Stripe handles card and on-chain payments, with a fee premium of approximately three percent absorbed into the quoted price. Mercury handles ACH direct debit for larger engagements where the fee premium is not justified. The customer chooses the rail at the point of payment. On receipt, payment confirmation writes back to the Supabase record and triggers the engagement kickoff workflow through Make.com.

4.9 Merkle accountability chain

Every consequential event in the scenario lifecycle writes a record to an append-only log. The log is structured as a hash chain: each record's hash includes the hash of the prior record, so no record can be silently altered without breaking the chain forward of the alteration. Periodic root hashes are published to Base for public verifiability. The chain is confirmation infrastructure; it references the Supabase record but does not replace it.

05 · Non-functional requirements

How the engine should behave.

The engine loads in under two seconds on a typical connection and functions entirely on the client for the composition phase, so visitors can explore scenarios without any backend round-trip until they request a pre-authorized invoice. Every form control is keyboard accessible. Color contrast meets WCAG AA across the interface. The visual language follows the co-op.us design system.

The engine degrades gracefully. If the availability query fails, the engine proceeds but marks the scenario as capacity-pending. If the hash chain service is unavailable, the Supabase record is still written and the chain reconciles on recovery. If Stripe or Mercury is down, the invoice remains valid and the customer is offered an alternative payment window.

06 · Data model

The records the engine creates.

The engine creates and maintains records across four primary tables in Supabase. Each table has row-level security tuned to the access pattern. Customers see only their own scenarios; members see only their own capacity commitments; operations staff see the full set.

08 · Acceptance criteria

What counts as done.

  1. A visitor can compose a scenario. Starting from techne.institute/build, a visitor can name a project, select a pattern, adjust complexity and infrastructure tier, choose an ongoing horizon, and see live totals update as they move.
  2. The labor breakdown is legible. Each role in the allocation carries a one-sentence explanation of what the role contributes. The total labor hours are the sum of the role hours. Clicking a role reveals a further explanation.
  3. The infrastructure breakdown is legible. Each line item in the infrastructure profile names a real service or provisioning choice and carries an estimated monthly cost. The total monthly infrastructure is the sum of the line items.
  4. A visitor can request a pre-authorized invoice. Completing a scenario writes a record to Supabase, generates a PDF invoice with a hash-linked URL, emails the PDF to the customer, and surfaces the pending invoice to operations staff.
  5. Operations can countersign. An operations staff member can mark an invoice as approved, which transitions it to payable and sends the customer a payment link.
  6. A customer can pay. From the payment link, the customer chooses Stripe or Mercury, completes payment, and receives a confirmation. The scenario record reflects the payment and the engagement is scheduled.
  7. The chain is verifiable. Every event in the scenario lifecycle writes a hash to the chain log. A root hash is published to Base at configurable cadence. A public verifier page at techne.institute/build/verify lets anyone submit a record hash and confirm its presence in the chain.
  8. Members see their commitments. A signed-in member viewing their private dashboard sees their current capacity commitments, pending pre-authorized invoices referencing their capacity, and active engagement schedule.
09 · Scope exclusions

What the engine is not.

The engine is not a project management tool. Once an engagement is active, the work is tracked elsewhere. The engine is not a contract drafting surface. The pre-authorized invoice references a statement of work but does not generate it; statements of work are drafted in the cooperative's agreements repository. The engine is not an internal compensation system. The customer-facing FMV pricing and the internal patronage allocation are distinct surfaces, deliberately kept separate because they answer different questions.

The engine does not attempt to price every possible digital system. It prices the patterns the cooperative has built before and can confidently build again. A visitor whose project does not fit any pattern is offered a planning conversation rather than an extrapolated number.

10 · Open questions

Still to be resolved.

Rate adjustment for aligned organizations

policy
Whether cooperatives, nonprofits, and civic organizations receive an adjustment at the engine layer, or whether the adjustment is applied at the countersignature stage by operations staff.

Member representation in the estimate

visibility
Whether the scenario preview shows the names of likely assigned members, or keeps the assignment opaque until countersignature. There are arguments on both sides: legibility of the labor pool against the member's right to decline specific engagements.

Chain publication cadence

infra
How often merkle roots are published to Base. More frequent means stronger public guarantees but higher gas costs. A daily rollup with immediate attestation for payment events is a reasonable starting position.

Fallback for pattern mismatch

ux
When a visitor's project does not fit any library pattern, how the engine handles the conversation handoff. An embedded intake form that captures the scenario as drafted, rather than a bounce to email, preserves context for the planning conversation.
11 · The learner surface

The tool as pedagogy.

A visitor who never intends to commission work should still leave the engine having learned something. The engine embeds explanations at every decision point. A hover on "DevOps" explains what the role actually does. A click on "managed PaaS" opens a short plain-language description of what a Platform-as-a-Service is and why it costs what it costs. A tooltip on the complexity multiplier explains what complexity means in practice and why small changes to a product surface often imply large changes to the labor pool underneath.

The engine is the first instrument of the Institute's broader commitment: that legibility is the condition under which ordinary people can make informed decisions about the digital tools they commission, operate, or inherit. The engine should leave a visitor more capable of reasoning about digital work, regardless of whether an invoice is ever generated.
