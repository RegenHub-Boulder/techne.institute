A vision for the crafts

What you shape — and what shapes you

Techne recognizes eight fundamental crafts — ways of working that have existed in every culture and every century. Nodes form where crafts meet place. The network is built on what people actually do.

The elements

Eight Ways of Making

Every person who enters a Techne node carries two crafts: a primary — what you shape — and a secondary — what shapes you. The combination is your archetype: the specific orientation you bring to any work, any community, any problem.

These are not job titles. They are not credentials. They are ways of engaging with the world that predate any industry and outlast any technology. A farmer, a fermentation scientist, and a landscape architect all practice Earth. A nurse, a mediator, and a community organizer all practice Water. The craft is deeper than the role.

The intersections

Sixty-Four Archetypes

Primary craft × secondary craft = your archetype. Not a label imposed from outside but a recognition of how your crafts combine. 8 × 8 = 64 unique archetypes — each one a distinct orientation to the work of making.

The archetype names reach into the history of craft itself. They are not metaphors. A Terraformer (Code × Earth) really does build digital systems informed by ecological patterns. A Midwife (Body × Water) really does bring things into being through presence and care. The names honor what the intersection actually produces.

The Eight Pure Archetypes

On the diagonal of the matrix sit the pure archetypes — those whose primary and secondary craft are the same. The Logician (Code × Code): systems all the way down. The Grower (Earth × Earth): life tending life. The Firekeeper (Fire × Fire): transformation sustaining itself. The Wellkeeper (Water × Water): connection nourishing connection. These are the anchors of each guild — the practitioners who go deepest rather than widest.

Where craft meets place

Nodes Form Where Crafts Converge

A Techne node is not a generic coworking space that happens to have a craft vocabulary. It is a place where specific crafts converge around specific local conditions. The crafts present in a node shape its character, its programming, its economy, and its contribution to the network.

Some nodes form around a single dominant craft — a workshop town, a music scene, a farming collective. Others form at the intersection of two or three crafts that discover they need each other. The most generative nodes attract all eight, creating the full ecology of making. But every node starts with a specific convergence, not an abstract mission.

Between Nodes

When nodes in different cities share a craft, they form guild bridges — living connections between the Guild of the Root in Boulder and the Guild of the Root in Oaxaca. Not a corporate partnership. A community of practice that spans geography. The farmer in one city and the farmer in another are already kin. The guild bridge makes that kinship functional: shared knowledge, portable reputation, exchange residencies, collaborative projects.

And when nodes with different dominant crafts connect, the network produces something no single node could: the Luthier in Detroit needs the Stonecutter in Carrara. The Navigator in Nairobi has a coordination pattern that the Civic Studio in Boulder needs. Craft diversity across nodes is the network’s immune system — the guarantee that the whole is more capable than any part.

The living economy

Craft as Economic Identity

In the Techne network, your craft is not a hobby category. It is your economic identity — the basis on which you find collaborators, get hired, build a track record, and contribute to your node’s productive capacity.

Evidence, not credentials. Your archetype is not a badge you receive on day one and carry forever. It is a living record of what you demonstrably do — contributions observed, work produced, capability developed over time. A Terraformer who has spent two years building ecological data systems at the Boulder node carries that record to any node in the network. Reputation is earned, portable, and queryable.

Craft evolution. People change. A Chronicler (Word × Code) discovers a calling in Earth and becomes a Seedkeeper (Earth × Word). The network remembers all past archetypes — your history of transformation is itself a form of craft. Reforging is not failure. It is the deepest expression of techne: the capacity to become something new through practice.

Local circulation. When a node needs a capability, it queries its own craft composition first. The Food Lab needs a website? The Chronicler three desks away can build it. The Civic Studio needs someone who understands soil data? The Terraformer in the same building has been thinking about exactly that. Craft identity makes the local economy legible to itself — matchmaking that no algorithm needs to mediate, because the people are already in the same room.

The case

Why Craft Matters Now

Automation is coming for tasks. It is not coming for craft. A task is something a system can perform. A craft is a relationship between a human, a material, a community, and a tradition. The distinction is the most important one in the economy ahead.

The word techne predates the split between art and engineering, between making and thinking, between manual and intellectual work. When the Greeks used it, they meant the whole thing: the potter’s knowledge of clay, the shipwright’s knowledge of wood and water, the healer’s knowledge of the body in its environment. Productive knowledge inseparable from the practitioner who holds it.

We are entering a period when this older understanding becomes practically urgent. AI can perform tasks at scale. It cannot practice a craft. It cannot form the relationships within which craft produces value. It cannot tend a substrate. The economy that emerges on the other side of automation will be organized around what humans do that requires presence, relationship, judgment, and place — which is to say, around craft.

Techne builds the infrastructure for that economy before it fully arrives. The crafts are the ontology. The nodes are the institutions. The guilds are the communities of practice. The network is the connective tissue. And the substrate test — does this regenerate or debase the relationships, skills, and local capacity it arises from? — is the discipline that keeps the whole thing honest.

What Is Your Craft?

Everyone carries at least two. The one you shape the world with and the one that shapes you back. The Techne network is built on the conviction that those crafts — named, practiced, composed — are the foundation of any economy worth building.

Sixty-four archetypes. Eight guilds. Nodes wherever crafts converge around a place and a purpose. A network that connects them without extracting from them.

The craft is the unit. The node is the institution. The network is the commons.
