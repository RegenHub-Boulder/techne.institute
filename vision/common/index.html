The common vision

What We Hold Together

PIE *ko-moin- · Latin communis · “held in common, shared by all”

Not shared property. Shared capacity — the ability to hold something together across time, tended by the people who depend on it.

Techne builds five dimensions of commons into a network of locally owned institutions. Craft, civic life, knowledge, economy, and ecology — held together in specific places by specific people.

The erosion

What Happens When the Commons Disappears

A commons is not an abstraction. It is a specific, tended thing — a fishery, a shared irrigation system, a public library, a neighborhood where people know each other’s names. It exists because someone maintains it and everyone who depends on it participates in its governance. When the commons disappears, what replaces it is not freedom but fragmentation.

Over the last half-century, commons in every dimension have been eroded. Craft knowledge concentrates in proprietary platforms. Civic gathering spaces close or commercialize. Local knowledge drains into global databases owned by distant shareholders. Economic value extracts before it circulates. Ecological systems are treated as externalities rather than substrates.

The result is a society rich in information and poor in capacity. People with access to all the world’s knowledge who cannot coordinate with their neighbors. Cities full of skilled individuals who cannot find each other. Economies that generate enormous wealth that never touches the ground it was generated from.

The diagnosis is not technological. The technology exists. The diagnosis is institutional: we have lost the forms — the cooperatives, the guilds, the commons, the third places — that turn individual capability into shared capacity. Techne rebuilds those forms.

Five dimensions

The Dimensions of a Living Commons

A Techne node is not a single-purpose institution. It is a commons in five dimensions — each one essential, each one reinforcing the others. Remove any one and the others weaken. Develop all five and the commons becomes self-sustaining: a living system that regenerates the substrate it arises from.

Dimension I

The Craft Commons

The knowledge that lives in making — shared between practitioners, accumulated across generations, held in guilds rather than corporations.

Eight fundamental crafts — Code, Word, Form, Sound, Earth, Body, Fire, Water — name the ways humans have always shaped the world. Each practitioner carries two: a primary (what you shape) and a secondary (what shapes you). The combination produces sixty-four archetypes, each one a distinct orientation to work.

The craft commons is the knowledge that flows between practitioners in the same guild — the soil scientist teaching the farmer, the senior developer reviewing the junior’s code, the master potter showing where the clay wants to go. This knowledge cannot be extracted into a database. It transmits through proximity, demonstration, and shared practice.

In practice: Apprenticeship programs within guilds. Cross-craft collaboration in convergence nodes. Portable reputation built on demonstrated work, not credentials. Craft evolution as a record of growth, not a career pivot.
Dimension II

The Civic Commons

The infrastructure for gathering, coordinating, and acting together — the third place that makes a city legible to itself.

Before the internet, every neighborhood had places where civic life happened informally — the barbershop, the town square, the public house. These spaces coordinated local knowledge without anyone calling it coordination. You knew who was hiring, who needed help, what was happening this weekend, because you were in the room where that information lived.

A Techne node restores this function at a scale appropriate to a modern city. Not a nostalgic recreation of village life but a serious institutional form — cooperatively owned, democratically governed, open to the whole city — that serves as the Schelling point where people go when they want to find what their community is doing.

In practice: Programming that makes demand exceed capacity. A local discovery layer. Events, salons, classes, cultural programming. The default answer to “what is happening tonight?”
Dimension III

The Knowledge Commons

Collective memory — the community’s accumulated observations, decisions, and insights, queryable by everyone who contributes to it.

Every community generates enormous amounts of knowledge: who is building what, what has been tried before, which approaches worked, what skills exist, what opportunities are open. In most communities, this knowledge is scattered across individual minds, private messages, and forgotten meeting notes. It has no structure, no persistence, and no way to be queried.

The knowledge commons gives this collective memory a home. Not a database people are forced to update but the natural byproduct of people working together with shared tools in a shared space. Observations accumulate. Patterns become visible. The community becomes capable of asking — and answering — questions about itself.

In practice: Contribution records built from observed work. Skill maps that update through participation. Shared project archives. A community that can query its own knowledge: “Who here knows Rust?” “Has anyone solved this problem before?” “What happened the last time we tried this?”
Dimension IV

The Economic Commons

Value that circulates locally before it extracts — cooperative capital, patronage accounting, and economic loops that close within the community.

The dominant economic pattern extracts value from local communities into global platforms. A freelancer in Boulder earns money through a platform headquartered in San Francisco that takes 20%. A local business pays rent to a landlord who lives in another state. The wealth generated in a place drains out before it can compound.

The economic commons reverses this flow. Members hire each other first. Revenue circulates through the cooperative before it leaves. Patronage accounting tracks every form of contribution — labor, capital, revenue, community — and distributes surplus to the people who generated it. The node is an economic engine, not a cost center.

In practice: Cooperative ownership with transparent capital accounts. Patronage-based surplus distribution. Venture incubation within the node. Local purchasing preferences. A treasury that compounds community wealth rather than extracting it.
Dimension V

The Ecological Commons

The ground everything else arises from — watershed, soil, season, the living systems that sustain the community and that the community must sustain in return.

Every Techne node exists in a specific ecology. Boulder sits where the Great Plains meet the Rocky Mountains, its water drawn from snowmelt, its agriculture shaped by altitude and aridity. Detroit sits on the Great Lakes, with different water, different soil, different possibilities. The ecological commons is the recognition that a node is not just in a place but of it — shaped by its conditions, accountable to its systems, oriented by its seasons.

This is the deepest substrate. Relationships, skills, economy, and knowledge all arise from a living world that has its own rhythms and constraints. A commons that ignores its ecological context is building on sand. A commons that integrates it — that tracks watershed health alongside financial health, that programs by season, that sources locally, that measures regeneration alongside revenue — is building on bedrock.

In practice: Bioregional data infrastructure. Programming tied to seasonal rhythms. Local sourcing for food and materials. Ecological health indicators alongside financial ones. The node as a place where people develop relationship with the specific land they live on.

The framework

A Framework Older Than It Looks

In 1962, Douglas Engelbart described five elements that co-evolve to produce human capability. He was designing a research lab. But the framework applies to any community — and maps precisely onto the five dimensions of the commons.

H
Human
Craft Commons. The human practitioner — skilled, embodied, carrying two crafts. The irreducible unit of capability.
L
Language
Knowledge Commons. Shared language, collective memory, the ability to query what the community knows. Without shared language, there is no shared capacity.
A
Artifacts
Civic Commons. The institutions, spaces, and tools that enable coordination. The node itself is the primary artifact — a third place built for collective action.
M
Methodology
Economic Commons. How value is generated, tracked, and distributed. Patronage accounting, cooperative governance, and the substrate test are all methodologies for economic coordination.
T
Training
Craft Commons. Capability development through practice in community. Apprenticeship, cohorts, the slow accumulation of skill through proximity to other practitioners.
e
Ecology
Ecological Commons. Techne’s addition to Engelbart. The living systems that sustain the community. Watershed, soil, season. The ground everything else depends on.

Engelbart’s deepest insight was not that tools augment humans. It was that the process of augmentation itself can be augmented — a community that uses its own tools to improve its own coordination gets better at getting better. He called this bootstrapping. It is the mechanism by which a commons, once established, can compound its own capacity across time.

The composition

How the Dimensions Compose

The five dimensions are not a checklist. They are a living system — each one feeding the others, each one incomplete without the rest. The commons is what emerges when all five are present in the same place.

Craft + Civic

Practitioners gathering in a third place. The guild meeting in the public house. This is the oldest pattern — craft knowledge shared in civic space. Neither functions without the other: craft without civic is isolation; civic without craft is entertainment.

Knowledge + Economic

Collective memory informing resource allocation. The community knows who contributes what, so patronage can track real value. The knowledge commons makes the economic commons honest — no one can claim what they didn’t produce.

Ecological + Craft

Practice grounded in place. The farmer’s knowledge of this specific soil. The builder’s knowledge of this climate. Craft becomes real when it answers to the conditions of a specific ecology. Otherwise it floats.

Civic + Economic

Democratic governance of shared resources. The cooperative form — one member, one vote — applied to the economic engine. The community decides how surplus is distributed, what programs to fund, which ventures to incubate. Economy accountable to polity.

Knowledge + Ecological

The community’s awareness of its own bioregion. Watershed data, seasonal patterns, soil health — integrated into the knowledge commons alongside skills and projects. A city that knows its own ecology can respond to it.

All Five Together

A place where practitioners of specific crafts gather in civic space, develop shared knowledge, generate cooperative economy, and ground it all in the living ecology of their bioregion. This is the commons. This is what Techne builds.

The discipline

The Test Applied to Each Dimension

The substrate test — does this regenerate or debase what it arises from? — applies differently in each dimension. A commons that passes the test in four dimensions but fails in one is incomplete. The discipline is total.

Craft: Do people leave more skilled than they arrived? Does the guild transmit real knowledge, or does it gatekeep? Is craft evolution honored as growth, not punished as inconsistency?

Civic: Does the space serve the whole city, or has it become a club? Does the programming weave ecosystems together, or compete with them? Is the door genuinely open?

Knowledge: Is the collective memory accessible to all participants? Does the knowledge commons grow through use, or does it require maintenance no one performs? Does it respect what is private while sharing what is common?

Economic: Does surplus circulate locally before it extracts? Does the patronage formula reflect real contribution? Are the people who generate value the people who govern its distribution?

Ecological: Is the node aware of its bioregion? Does its activity regenerate or deplete the local ecology? Does it program by season, source locally, and measure what matters beyond revenue?

The network

Commons Connected

When multiple nodes exist, their commons dimensions connect — producing a network that is itself a higher-order commons.

Craft commons connect through guild bridges — the Guild of the Root in one city sharing practice with the Guild of the Root in another. Knowledge flows between practitioners who share an orientation even when their conditions differ.

Civic commons connect through shared governance patterns — the grammar of how to form a cooperative, run a node, and apply the substrate test. Not a franchise. A shared language for institution-building that each community speaks in its own voice.

Knowledge commons connect through federated memory — each node maintains its own collective knowledge, but the network makes cross-node queries possible. “Who in the network has solved this problem?”

Economic commons connect through portable reputation and cross-node collaboration. A practitioner’s contribution record travels with them. Nodes can form economic partnerships without intermediaries.

Ecological commons connect through bioregional awareness — the recognition that watersheds, climate zones, and migration patterns cross political boundaries. Nodes in the same bioregion share ecological context. The network as a whole develops a relationship with the planet’s living systems.

What Holds Together

The commons is not an ideology. It is the oldest technology for managing shared capacity — older than the market, older than the state, and more resilient than either. Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Prize for proving what fishing villages and alpine meadow communities had known for centuries: that people can govern shared resources without privatizing or nationalizing them, if the institutional form is right.

Techne is an institutional form for the commons of the twenty-first century — where the shared resources include craft knowledge, civic infrastructure, collective memory, cooperative capital, and ecological ground. Five dimensions, held together in specific places by specific people, connected through a network that serves its nodes rather than extracting from them.

The craft is the unit. The node is the institution. The commons is what holds.
