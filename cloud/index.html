Infrastructure Research R13

Compute as Commons

A solar-powered local compute cooperative at 1515 Walnut. Service credits backed by physical infrastructure — issued as cooperative patronage, redeemable as a claim on capacity.

The word cloud has always been a polite fiction — a way of not saying "someone else's computers." The data centers that hold our work, our messages, our money are physical things: machines in buildings, drawing power from a grid, owned by a small number of large corporations.

Techne is building something different. On the rooftop of 1515 Walnut Street in Boulder, Colorado — the same building where RegenHub operates its cooperative workspace — we are proposing a solar-powered local compute array. Not a metaphor. Actual hardware: Mac Mini compute nodes, network-attached storage, and photovoltaic panels on the roof above them.

The compute this array produces will be issued as cooperative service credits — CLOUD credits — through the patronage accounting system of RegenHub, LCA. One CLOUD credit represents a $0.10 claim on cooperative compute capacity. Credits are earned by contributing to the cooperative, purchased at par, or received as patronage distributions. They are redeemed against actual services: compute time, storage, bandwidth.

The Postage Stamp

A postage stamp is not a promise to deliver something someday. It is a pre-paid, transferable claim on a specific service issued by the entity that controls delivery. Its face value tracks the cost of that service. You can hold it, trade it, redeem it — but only the postal cooperative can honor it.

This is exactly what a CLOUD credit is. The LCA controls the infrastructure. The credit is issued against capacity the cooperative actually has. Redemption is a claim on services the cooperative actually delivers. The accounting is open — patronage allocations are recorded in each member's capital account, visible in the cooperative ledger.

This model is the intersection of two things happening at Techne simultaneously: the CLOUD credit (RegenHub's service unit, initially a floating abstraction) and postage.protocol (an LCA organizer venture building the protocol layer for proof-backed local service claims). Together they become something more concrete: a mutual credit system backed by physical compute, auditable on-chain, governed by Colorado cooperative law.

Capacity attestation as primitive. The cooperative attests to its hardware capacity on-chain. Credits are issued against that attestation. Redemptions produce proofs. The full lifecycle — issuance, transfer, redemption, proof — is observable without requiring trust in any central party.

Why This is Distinct

Most stablecoin and tokenized-asset frameworks assume fiat reserves or financial instruments as backing. CLOUD credits are different on two counts:

Service backing, not fiat backing. The value is not "we hold $1 for every token." It is "we have physical hardware that can deliver $0.10 of compute per credit." Face value tracks service cost, not exchange rate.

Cooperative law as governance layer. Colorado's Limited Cooperative Association Act (§7-58-301 et seq.) provides the legal structure: infrastructure owned as patron capital, services delivered as patronage allocations, member accounts as the ledger. The LCA is the legal entity that stands behind each credit. This intersection with GENIUS Act / CLOUD Token Safe Harbor analysis (R9) suggests a novel classification path for cooperative service claims.

Locality as the primitive. The infrastructure is physically located in Boulder, CO. The members it serves are in the same bioregion. This locality — verifiable through attestation, anchored in property and cooperate law — is the primitive that makes "local cloud" meaningful rather than merely rhetorical.

As a Research Case Study

Techne is treating R13 as a living research case study in cooperative infrastructure economics. The questions we are investigating:

  • Can a Colorado LCA issue service credits that function as stamps (pre-paid, transferable, face-value claims) without triggering securities classification?
  • What attestation format — hardware inventory, solar output, uptime SLA — is sufficient to back a claim on-chain?
  • How does the patronage accounting treatment (Subchapter T / IRC 1385) interact with on-chain issuance and transfer?
  • Can locality itself be a verifiable primitive — something provable about where compute lives, not just who operates it?

The prototype will be built at the intersection of three active projects: the Techne intranet (patronage accounting UI), the postage.protocol (stamp issuance and redemption layer), and the 1515 Walnut build-out (physical infrastructure).

The goal is not to build a new token. The goal is to make a cooperative's physical capacity legible, transferable, and trustless — starting with the most concrete possible form: a box on a shelf, drawing power from the sun, doing work that the community actually needs done.

Scenario modeling, financial projections, and LCA patronage accounting breakdowns are available to members in the intranet scenario model.
