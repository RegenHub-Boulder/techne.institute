P330 — Formation Narrative

How RegenHub Came to Be

The word techne (τέχνη) means craft — not technique, not technology. The craft of recognizing which patterns a situation requires and composing them with care.

IBefore Filing: The Conditions

The Place

1515 Walnut Street, Boulder, Colorado. Third floor. By late 2025, a group of practitioners occupied the space — software engineers, designers, community builders, investors — working on separate projects but sharing tools, clients, and increasingly, a way of thinking about work. They were not a company. They were a scenius: Brian Eno's word for the collective intelligence that emerges from sustained proximity of culturally aligned people.

The Great Plains meet the Rocky Mountains here. Boulder sits at 5,430 feet in the South Boulder Creek watershed, in the shadow of the Flatirons. This geography is not incidental. Techne is place-based. The building, the watershed, the seasonal rhythms of this specific Front Range location shape what the cooperative can and should become.

The Problem

The informal arrangement exposed everyone to risk. Who holds liability when "my client" and "our space" blur? How does the group access capital without concentrating ownership? Traditional incorporation — C-corp, S-corp — concentrates governance in founders. That felt wrong for a group that had developed democratic habits: rotating facilitation, consensus-seeking, shared resources.

The Discovery

Colorado's Limited Cooperative Association statute (ULCAA, C.R.S. Title 7, Art. 58) was designed precisely for this problem. Passed through the work of Linda Phillips and Jason Weiner, it enables cooperatives to accept outside investment through non-voting membership classes while preserving democratic governance — one member, one vote.

This was the solution: a cooperative venture studio. Not a company that does things, but infrastructure that enables ventures to grow.

IIIThe Architecture

Soil, Not Plant

The central metaphor crystallized early: RegenHub is soil, not plant. A music label isn't a band. A venture studio isn't a startup. The cooperative provides infrastructure — space, legal structure, operational systems, capital access — for autonomous ventures to grow, mature, and eventually exit into their own structures.

*Class 4 members gain voting rights only if they also hold a patron-class interest. Multi-class membership is explicitly permitted.

Subchapter K

The cooperative elected partnership tax treatment under Subchapter K of the Internal Revenue Code — a deliberate departure from the Meadow source template's Subchapter T approach. Sub K provides loss pass-through to members during formation years, flexible allocation via IRC 704(b) capital accounts, and the capital account precision essential for a venture studio with varying contribution types.

Revenue Architecture

Three revenue streams sustain the infrastructure: space revenue (coworking, studio rental, events), service revenue (coordination infrastructure, formation assistance, consulting), and venture reciprocity (1% commitment from each venture, plus 10% equity gift into the cooperative's venture basket).

IVThe Capital Question

The Tension

Cooperatives have historically struggled with capital access. Traditional investors want equity, control, and exit liquidity — things that conflict with democratic governance.

The Resolution

Colorado's LCA statute offers a path through. Investor members hold a distinct membership class with defined financial rights but no governance control.

On February 20, 2026, Aaron G Neyer and Todd Youngblood walked for 29 minutes in Boulder and arrived at the term sheet shape:

"This capital contribution comes with no defined return to the investor. Any such return will be discussed and approved by the member body at a future date."

IXWhy It Matters

Most accounting tools are designed for extraction. They track what's taken, not what's contributed. They make competitive relationships legible and mutualistic ones invisible. The Economic Habitat Matrix maps this: governance orientation (dispersive to concentrative) against systemic relationship (extractive to contributive). Most organizations operate in the Competitive zone because that's what their tools can see.

RegenHub exists to make the Contributive and Mutualistic zones operable. Not through ideology, but through infrastructure: legal structure (LCA), economic tracking (patronage engine), coordination surface (Workshop), and place-based grounding (Boulder, the watershed, the seasonal rhythms of this specific geography).

The tools an organization uses to track value shape what it can see. What it can see determines whether it extracts or enriches. Techne is building the sensory apparatus for economic ecology.
