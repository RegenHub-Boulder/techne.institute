The naming

A Name Is Not Neutral

In 1955, John McCarthy coined "Artificial Intelligence" to get funding for a summer workshop at Dartmouth. It was a marketing strategy that hardened into a myth. The name planted an idea: that intelligence is something manufactured, something synthetic, something that lives in a machine. It oriented an entire field toward simulation — building systems that mimic individual human cognition.

There is another name for the same capability. Collective Intelligence — intelligence that is collective in origin (trained on the harvest of countless contributors) and collective in purpose (designed to help people think together, not to simulate a person thinking alone). This name orients toward amplification — extending what groups of humans can perceive, decide, and build.

The distinction is not cosmetic. It determines the institutional form. If you believe you are building an artificial mind, you optimize for replacement — the machine that does what the human did, cheaper and faster. If you believe you are cultivating collective intelligence, you optimize for composition — the system of humans and tools that accomplishes what neither could alone.

AI
Artificial Intelligence
Latin artificialis — made by art or skill, as opposed to natural

Intelligence as product. Something manufactured, owned, and deployed. The implicit frame: the machine is the agent. The human is the customer, the subject, or the obstacle.

  • Orientation Replacement
  • Unit of value The model
  • Ownership Shareholders
  • Question asked What can we automate?
  • Success metric Headcount reduced
  • Failure mode Dependency, displacement
CI
Collective Intelligence
Latin collectivus — gathered together; intelligentia — understanding between

Intelligence as relationship. Something that arises when conditions are right — like bread rising, like a watershed finding the sea. The implicit frame: the system is the agent. The humans and the tools are participants.

  • Orientation Amplification
  • Unit of value The composition
  • Ownership Members / contributors
  • Question asked What can we accomplish together?
  • Success metric Capability multiplied
  • Failure mode Fragility, under-investment

The bridge

Why Physical Space Matters

The AI path is frictionless by design. Models deployed through APIs. Workers displaced through Slack messages. Value extracted through screens. The entire trajectory is optimized for the virtual — for weightlessness, for placelessness, for the elimination of everything that makes work situated.

The CI path requires friction. It requires people in the same room, reading each other's faces, building trust through proximity, disagreeing in real time, sharing a meal after a hard meeting. It requires a building with south-facing windows and afternoon light. It requires a watershed. It requires a place.

This is not sentimentality. It is structural. The cooperative form depends on relationships — on members who know each other, govern together, and hold each other accountable. These relationships are possible online but they are durable in person. Every successful cooperative movement in history has been rooted in a physical community: Rochdale's weavers, Mondragón's Basque towns, Emilia-Romagna's craft networks.

The transition from AI to CI therefore requires a bridge — an institution that operates in both the virtual and the physical, that deploys computational capability while remaining rooted in a place, a community, a set of face-to-face relationships that no API can replicate.

Theory of Change

If we build the bridge, the path from AI to CI becomes walkable.

If

Name the mechanism, not just the fear

The threat is not technology. It is the institutional form that makes replacement the default. When people understand this, the response shifts from panic to design. Fear becomes a blueprint.

And

Build a working prototype in physical space

A cooperative venture studio that deploys AI capability for member benefit — with a building, a lease, a local economy, face-to-face governance. Not a whitepaper. A place you can visit.

And

Prove amplification outperforms extraction

Measure it. Are members doing more meaningful work? Is surplus growing and distributed equitably? Are humans developing new capabilities alongside the tools? Publish the results — including the failures.

And

Make the pattern composable

Open-source the governance tools, the patronage formulas, the coordination infrastructure. Make it concretely easier for any community to form a cooperative that deploys CI rather than AI.

Then

The alternative becomes visible and replicable

One prototype proves the concept. Ten prototypes prove the pattern. A hundred prototypes change the policy landscape. The cooperative AI path becomes a real option, not a thought experiment.

So

Collective Intelligence becomes the default, not the exception

Not by defeating Artificial Intelligence — but by building institutions so demonstrably better for the humans inside them that the extractive path becomes the one that requires justification.

How We Know It's Working

Meaningful work index

Are members spending more time on judgment, creativity, and relationship — and less on tasks that AI handles better?

Surplus distribution

Is the cooperative's patronage formula distributing gains to contributors? Is the gap between highest and lowest allocation narrowing?

Capability co-evolution

Are humans learning new skills alongside the tools? Are they composing with AI, not just consuming its output?

Institutional resilience

Does the cooperative hold under pressure? When revenue drops, does it protect members or sacrifice them? The stress test reveals the structure.

Replication rate

How many other communities have used the pattern? Not downloads — functioning cooperatives with physical space and real governance.

Place health

Is the bioregion better for Techne's presence? Local spending, ecological monitoring, community infrastructure. CI should enrich its watershed, not just its balance sheet.

The choice

AI or CI

The capability is here. The models exist. The question that remains is not technical but institutional: do we organize this capability as Artificial Intelligence — manufactured, owned, deployed for extraction — or as Collective Intelligence — cultivated, shared, governed by the people it serves?

The AI path is easier. It is funded. It has momentum. It is the default trajectory of every incentive structure in the current economy.

The CI path is harder. It requires building institutions — cooperatives, commons, governance structures — that take years to mature. It requires physical space, face-to-face trust, and the patience to let relationships compound. It requires choosing amplification over replacement every time the extractive shortcut presents itself.

But the CI path has one structural advantage the AI path cannot match: the people inside it want to be there. A cooperative where members govern their own relationship with technology generates loyalty, creativity, and resilience that no amount of capital can purchase. This is not a moral argument. It is a competitive one.

The bread rises because the baker creates the conditions. The institution is the bakery. The question is whether we build bakeries or factories.

Techne is testing this in practice. A cooperative venture studio in Boulder, Colorado — physical space, virtual infrastructure, collective intelligence deployed for member benefit. The experiment is public. The books are open. The invitation stands.
